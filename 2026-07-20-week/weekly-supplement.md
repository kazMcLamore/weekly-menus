# PART 1 — SHOPPING GUIDE

## Understanding This Week's Ingredients

This is a Southern American BBQ menu — very different from Mexican cuisine. Many ingredients are unfamiliar in a Mexican supermarket. Here's what each thing is, what it looks like, what it tastes like, and where to find it.

---

### 🥬 Collard Greens (coles / berza)

**What is it?** A large leafy green — looks like quelites (lamb's quarters) but the leaves are much bigger, about the size of a dinner plate. Dark green, thick leaves with a sturdy central stem.

**What does it taste like?** Earthy and slightly bitter when raw, similar to quelites or kale (col rizada). When cooked low and slow with bacon fat and vinegar, it becomes tender, slightly tangy, and absorbs the smoky bacon flavor. Nothing like spinach — much heartier, holds its texture.

**Where to find it in Playa del Carmen:** Probably the hardest ingredient this week. Collard greens are a Southern US staple — they are NOT commonly sold in Mexican supermarkets. Try:
1. **DAC Market** (30 Av. entre Calle 14 y 14 Bis) — specialises in imported ingredients, your best bet
2. **Chedraui Selecto** (Plaza Las Américas) — sometimes carries imported US produce

**Substitutions (in order of preference):**
1. **Acelgas (Swiss chard)** — closest in texture and flavour. Cooks faster (30 min instead of 1 hour). Readily available in any Mexican market.
2. **Col rizada (kale)** — slightly tougher, needs ~45 minutes. Available at Chedraui Selecto and sometimes Walmart.
3. **Quelites** — smaller leaves, more delicate. Cook only 20–25 minutes. Widely available in Playa del Carmen markets.
4. **Espinaca (spinach)** — Last resort. Very delicate, cooks in 10 minutes. Not ideal — will turn mushy if cooked 1 hour. Only use if nothing else is available.

🔴 Difficulty: **Hard** (imported, limited availability)

---

### 🥩 Pork Butt / Pork Shoulder (espaldilla de cerdo / cabeza de lomo)

**What is it?** A large, well-marbled cut from the upper part of the pig's front leg. It has a thick fat pad on one side and usually comes with a bone. Weighs about 2.3 kg / 5 lb. It's NOT a butt (trasero) — the name comes from old English "butt" meaning barrel, because this cut was packed in barrels. In Mexico, ask for "espaldilla de cerdo" or "cabeza de lomo."

**What does it taste like?** Rich, fatty, porky. When cooked low and slow for 12+ hours, the fat and collagen break down and the meat becomes shreddable — you pull it apart with two forks. The brine + dry rub give it a sweet, smoky, slightly spicy crust.

**Where to find it:** Every Mexican butcher and supermarket carries this cut. It's used for carnitas and cochinita pibil. Available at:
- Chedraui (Av. Juárez)
- Walmart (30 Av. y Calle 8)
- Mega Soriana (30 Av. y Constituyentes)

**What to ask the butcher:** "Espaldilla de cerdo con hueso, de aproximadamente 2 kilos y medio, con su capa de grasa." (Bone-in pork shoulder, about 2.5 kg, with the fat cap on.)

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy** (every supermarket has it)

---

### 🍖 Pork Ribs — costillar de cerdo

**What is it?** A rack of pork ribs — baby back (from the loin area, curved, smaller bones) or spare ribs (from the belly area, flatter, bigger bones with more fat). We need ONE full rack, about 1.8 kg / 4 lb. You want them as a single slab (tira entera), not cut into individual ribs.

**What does it taste like?** Sweet, smoky, porky. The dry rub (brown sugar, smoked paprika, garlic) forms a crust. The meat pulls cleanly off the bone. Finished with a vinegar-water dip (Memphis style).

**Where to find it in Playa del Carmen — ranked by likelihood:**
1. **Chedraui Selecto** (Plaza Las Américas) — best meat counter for US-style cuts. Look for vacuum-packed imported baby back ribs.
2. **Walmart** (30 Av. y Calle 8) — often carries frozen or refrigerated baby back ribs (imported, sometimes Smithfield brand)
3. **Mega Soriana** (30 Av. y Constituyentes) — ask at the meat counter for "costillar de cerdo para BBQ"

**What to ask the butcher:** "Costillar de cerdo, tira entera, de aproximadamente 2 kilos." If they only have it cut into individual ribs (costillas cortadas), that's OK — ask for "la tira entera, sin cortar" — but if they can't, individual ribs work too (just harder to remove the membrane).

**⚠️ BACKUP PLAN — Beef Burnt Ends:** If pork ribs are completely unavailable anywhere, switch to the backup dish: **Beef Burnt Ends** (puntas de carne de res caramelizadas). You'll need:
- **Diezmillo / chamorro de res** (beef chuck roast) — 1.4 kg / 3 lb. Widely available at ALL Mexican butchers.
- **Humo líquido** (liquid smoke) — 1 tsp. Available at Chedraui Selecto or DAC Market (small bottle, Wright's or Colgin brand).
- **Mostaza amarilla** (yellow mustard) — 2 tbsp. Any supermarket (French's is the standard).
- **Sal de ajo** (garlic salt) — 1 tsp.
- **Sazón para carne** (steak seasoning) — 1 tsp. McCormick Montreal Steak or similar.

🟡 Difficulty: **Medium** (US-style racks may be special-order; individual ribs or backup chuck are always available)

---

### 🐔 BBQ Chicken Pieces

**What is it?** Bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs (muslos) and drumsticks (piernas). You want 10 pieces total — mix of thighs and drumsticks. The bone and skin are ESSENTIAL — boneless skinless won't work. The bone keeps it juicy; the skin goes crispy with the BBQ glaze.

**What does it taste like?** Juicy dark meat with a sweet, sticky, slightly charred BBQ sauce glaze. Three layers of BBQ sauce, each one baked on in 7-minute intervals, creating a thick, caramelised crust.

**Where to find it:** Available literally everywhere in Mexico. Any supermarket, any pollería.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy** (the most basic ingredient on the list)

---

### 🥓 Bacon (tocino)

**What is it?** Cured, smoked pork belly, sliced. NOT the same as Mexican "tocino" which is often unsmoked pork belly or pork fat. We need American-style smoked bacon — the kind that gets crispy.

You'll need **~1 kg total** = 150g for baked beans + 900g for collard greens. That's about 2 × 500g packs, or one large 1 kg pack.

**What does it taste like?** Smoky, salty, fatty. When cooked crispy, it adds crunch and smoke to collard greens. The rendered fat (grasa del tocino) is the FLAVOUR BASE for the greens — do NOT discard it.

**Where to find it:** Widely available in Mexican supermarkets. Brands: FUD, Zwan, Oscar Mayer, or any imported US brand at Chedraui Selecto. Look for "tocino ahumado" (smoked bacon) specifically.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy**

---

### 🥔 Russet / Idaho Potatoes (papas para puré)

**What is it?** Large, brown-skinned potatoes with white flesh. High starch, low moisture — these are the potatoes that make fluffy, creamy mashed potatoes. NOT the yellow waxy potatoes (papas amarillas/gallega) which are better for boiling whole or salads.

You need **4.1 kg / 9 lb** — this is a LOT. It will make ~17 cups of mashed potatoes.

**What to look for:** Brown skin, oval shape, heavy for their size. Sold as "papa blanca" or "papa americana" in Mexico. The bag might say "Russet" or "Idaho." If you can't find russets, "papa blanca" (standard white potato) works, but the texture will be slightly less fluffy.

**Where to find it:** Every supermarket in Mexico carries white potatoes. Chedraui Selecto and Walmart are most likely to have specifically labeled Russet/Idaho potatoes.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy**

---

### 🌽 Yellow Cornmeal (harina de maíz amarilla)

**⚠️ THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT INGREDIENT TO GET RIGHT ⚠️**

**What it IS:** Coarse, yellow, ground dried corn. It looks like fine sand or polenta — gritty and granular. It is NOT cornstarch. It is NOT masa harina.

**What it IS NOT — DO NOT BUY THESE:**
- ❌ **Fécula de maíz / Maicena** (cornstarch) — white, fine powder. Wrong. Your cornbread will be glue.
- ❌ **Masa harina** (nixtamalised corn flour for tortillas, like Maseca) — treated with lime/cal. Wrong. Different flavour and texture entirely.

**How to check the bag:** Read the ingredients. It should say ONE ingredient: "corn" or "maíz." Nothing else. The texture should feel gritty, not silky. The colour should be YELLOW, not white.

**Brand names to look for:**
- Bob's Red Mill "Cornmeal" / "Harina de Maíz Amarilla" (available at Chedraui Selecto)
- Indian Head Yellow Cornmeal (imported, sometimes at DAC Market)
- Any coarse-ground yellow cornmeal / polenta amarilla

**If you can't find yellow:** White cornmeal (harina de maíz blanca) works — the flavour is slightly less corny but the texture is identical. Same rule: coarse, one ingredient, NOT masa harina.

🟡 Difficulty: **Medium** (Chedraui Selecto international section or DAC Market have it; regular Chedraui may only carry masa harina)

---

### 🥫 Canned Cream Style Corn (elote en crema en lata)

**What is it?** Sweet corn that's been partially pureed into a creamy, milky consistency. It's a canned product — you don't make it yourself. Brand names: Del Monte "Cream Style Corn" or La Costeña "Elote en Crema."

**What does it look like?** Pale yellow, thick and porridge-like, with visible bits of corn kernel. NOT whole kernel corn — it's creamier and wetter. The can is usually labelled with a picture of corn and a creamy sauce.

**Where to find it:** Widely available in Mexican supermarkets. La Costeña and Del Monte both sell it. Look in the canned vegetable aisle, near the regular sweet corn.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy**

---

### 🍎 Apple Cider / Apple Juice for Brine (sidra de manzana / jugo de manzana)

**⚠️ IMPORTANT: This is apple CIDER (the drink), NOT apple cider VINEGAR (the acid) ⚠️**

**What it is:** Fresh, cloudy apple juice — unfiltered, brownish, sometimes with sediment at the bottom. It's the juice pressed from apples, not fermented into vinegar. In the US it's called "apple cider." In Mexico it's harder to find than clear apple juice.

**What to buy if real apple cider doesn't exist:** Regular clear apple juice (jugo de manzana claro, like Jumex or Del Valle) works perfectly for the brine. The cloudiness of real cider doesn't affect the brine — the sugar and apple flavour are what matter.

**Where to find it:** Any supermarket (jugo de manzana Jumex, Minute Maid, etc.). If you find actual "sidra de manzana" (cloudy, unfiltered, possibly sparkling), that's the real thing — use it.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy** (regular apple juice is fine)

---

### 🍎 Apple Cider Vinegar (vinagre de manzana)

**What is it?** Vinegar made from fermented apple juice. Light golden colour, fruity-tart flavour. It's THE foundational flavour of Southern cooking — used in the pulled pork brine, the Carolina vinegar sauce, the collard greens, and the baked beans.

**What does it taste like?** Milder and fruitier than white vinegar. Less harsh. Slightly sweet apple undertone under the sour.

**Where to find it:** Every supermarket in Mexico. Brands: Bragg's, Heinz, or Mexican brands (Vinagre de Manzana). Don't buy the expensive organic "with the mother" kind unless you want to — regular filtered apple cider vinegar works identically.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy**

---

### 🍯 Molasses (melaza)

**What is it?** A thick, dark, almost-black syrup that's a byproduct of sugar refining. It's NOT honey — much thicker, darker, and with a bitter-sweet, almost smoky flavour. Used in baked beans for that authentic Southern depth.

**What does it taste like?** Intensely dark, slightly bitter, mineral-sweet. Like burnt caramel mixed with iron. A little goes a long way — only 1/4 cup in the beans.

**What does it look like?** A thick, opaque, dark brown syrup — pours like cold honey. The bottle will be labelled "melaza" or "molasses." NOT "miel de caña" (cane syrup / golden syrup) — that's lighter and sweeter.

<img src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/41Kb6dsX9kL.jpg" width="200" alt="Molasses/Melaza bottle">

**Where to find it:**
1. **Chedraui** (Av. Juárez) — usually in the baking aisle or near sugars
2. **Walmart** (30 Av.) — imported US brands like Grandma's or Brer Rabbit
3. **Chedraui Selecto** — definitely has it in international/baking

**Substitutions (in order of preference):**
1. **Miel de caña oscura** (dark cane syrup / golden syrup) — closest substitute. Sweeter, less bitter.
2. **Miel de maple** (maple syrup) — works but completely different flavour profile. Use 1/3 cup.
3. **Azúcar morena** (brown sugar) — 1/3 cup packed. Sweetness but no depth. Beans will taste different.
4. **Piloncillo** dissolved in 2 tbsp hot water — closest Mexican substitute. Good depth but different flavour.

🟡 Difficulty: **Medium** (most larger supermarkets carry it; smaller ones may not)

---

### 🔴 Smoked Paprika (pimentón ahumado)

**What is it?** Dried red peppers that were smoked over oak wood, then ground into a fine red powder. It's NOT the same as regular paprika (pimentón dulce) — the "ahumado" (smoked) part is critical. It smells like a campfire. This is the ingredient that gives Southern BBQ food its signature "smoky" flavour without a smoker.

<img src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/51cdZVlK26L.jpg" width="200" alt="Smoked Paprika/Pimentón Ahumado">

**What does it taste like?** Smoky, slightly sweet, mild heat. Tastes like barbecue even without a grill. One whiff smells like a parrillada.

**Where to find it in Playa del Carmen:**
1. **Chedraui Selecto** (Plaza Las Américas) — international spice section. Brands: La Chinata, El Avion, McCormick.
2. **DAC Market** (30 Av. entre Calle 14 y 14 Bis) — imported spices section.
3. **Walmart** (30 Av.) — sometimes in the international aisle. McCormick "Smoked Paprika."

**If you can't find it:** Regular paprika (pimentón dulce) + 1/8 tsp liquid smoke per tbsp of paprika. But the real thing is much better.

🔴 Difficulty: **Hard** (specialty spice in Mexico; DAC Market or Chedraui Selecto international section)

---

### 🧂 Kosher Salt (sal kosher / sal de grano)

**What is it?** Coarse-grained salt with large, flat crystals. It's NOT "sal kosher para comida kosher" — it's called kosher salt because it's used in the koshering process of meats. It has no iodine, no anti-caking agents. The large flakes dissolve slower and season more evenly.

**What does it look like?** Large, irregular, flaky white crystals — like small snowflakes. Much coarser than regular table salt. Feels different between your fingers — more texture.

**Where to find it:**
- **Chedraui Selecto** (international section) — Diamond Crystal or Morton brands
- **DAC Market** — imported salts section
- **Amazon Mexico (amazon.com.mx)** — search "sal kosher"

**Substitutions:** Regular coarse sea salt (sal de mar en grano) works perfectly. The key is COARSE texture, not the label. Mexican "sal de grano" or "sal de mar gruesa" is functionally identical. If you only have fine table salt, use HALF the amount — fine salt is much denser.

🟡 Difficulty: **Medium** (Chedraui Selecto has it; coarse sea salt is an easy substitute everywhere)

---

### 🌿 Specialty Dried Herbs: Rubbed Sage, Marjoram, White Pepper

These are used in tiny amounts (1/4 tsp each) in the rib dry rub. They're nice to have but won't break the dish if missing.

**Rubbed Sage (salvia seca frotada):**
- What it is: Dried sage leaves that have been rubbed into a fluffy, cotton-like powder. NOT ground sage (which is fine like dust). Smells intensely herbal, slightly minty.
- Where to find it: DAC Market (imported spices). Amazon Mexico has McCormick Rubbed Sage.
- Substitutions: Ground sage (salvia molida) — use 1/8 tsp (half the amount, because ground is more concentrated). OR dried oregano + dried thyme mixed 50/50.
- 🔴 Difficulty: **Hard**

**Dried Marjoram (mejorana seca):**
- What it is: A delicate herb similar to oregano but sweeter, more floral. Common in Mexican cooking — often sold as "mejorana."
- Where to find it: Any Mexican supermarket spice section. Much easier than sage.
- Substitutions: Dried oregano (use same amount).
- 🟢 Difficulty: **Easy** (standard Mexican spice)

**White Pepper (pimienta blanca):**
- What it is: Black peppercorns with the outer black skin removed — the inner white seed. Milder, earthier, less sharp than black pepper. Used in light-coloured dishes where black specks would be visible.
- Where to find it: Most supermarkets carry it. McCormick white pepper is common.
- Substitutions: Black pepper (same amount) — flavour slightly different but functionally fine.
- 🟢 Difficulty: **Easy**

---

### 🥛 Buttermilk (suero de leche / buttermilk)

**What is it?** Tangy, thick, cultured milk. Traditionally it was the liquid left after churning butter. Today it's cultured skim milk — thick and sour, like a drinkable yogurt.

**Where to find it in Playa del Carmen:**
1. **Chedraui Selecto** (Plaza Las Américas) — dairy section, near milk and cream. Often imported US brands (Borden, etc.).
2. **Walmart** (30 Av.) — sometimes in the dairy section.

**If you can't find it — EASY DIY:** 1 cup regular milk + 1 tablespoon white vinegar. Stir. Let it sit for 10 minutes at room temperature. It will curdle slightly and thicken — that's your buttermilk. Works perfectly.

🟡 Difficulty: **Medium** (imported product; DIY substitute works perfectly)

---

### 🥫 Red Kidney Beans (frijoles rojos en lata)

**What are they?** Dark red, kidney-shaped beans in a can. NOT the same as black beans, pinto beans, or bayos. These are larger, firmer, and hold their shape during long simmering.

**Where to find them:** Nearly every Mexican supermarket. Brands: La Costeña, La Sierra, Del Monte. Look for "frijoles rojos" or "red kidney beans" on the English side of the can.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy**

---

### 🍅 Tomato Passata (puré de tomate)

**What is it?** Smooth, strained, uncooked tomato puree. NOT tomato paste (pasta de tomate — too concentrated). NOT tomato sauce / ketchup (catsup — has sugar and spices). Passata is just tomatoes, pureed and strained — no seasoning, no cooking.

**Where to find it:** Most Mexican supermarkets carry "puré de tomate" in tetra-pak boxes or jars. Brands: La Costeña, Del Fuerte, or imported Italian passata (Mutti, Cirio) at Chedraui Selecto.

**Substitutions:** Canned crushed tomatoes (tomate triturado en lata), blended smooth. OR fresh tomatoes, blended and strained — use 1 cup to replace 3/4 cup passata.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy**

---

### 🧴 Worcestershire Sauce (salsa inglesa)

**What is it?** A dark brown, thin, intensely savoury fermented sauce made from anchovies, tamarind, molasses, vinegar, and spices. It adds "umami" depth to the baked beans — you only need 1.5 tsp.

**⚠️ GLUTEN ALERT:** This is one of the FEW ingredients this week that may contain gluten. Many Mexican Worcestershire sauces (salsa inglesa) use soy sauce in their recipe — and soy sauce in Mexico often contains wheat. Brands to watch out for: La Costeña, some store brands.

**GF brands available in Mexico:**
- Lea & Perrins (the US formulation is GF — but the label may differ in Mexico. Check.)
- Some imported UK brands

**Substitutions if you can't find GF Worcestershire:**
1. **Tamari (salsa de soya japonesa sin trigo)** + a splash of vinegar — tamari is wheat-free soy sauce. Available at DAC Market or Chedraui Selecto.
2. **Omit entirely** + add 1/2 tsp extra salt + 1/4 tsp sugar. The beans will be slightly less complex but still good.
3. **Maggi seasoning** (verify GF on label — some formulations contain wheat).

🟡 Difficulty: **Medium** (the sauce itself is everywhere; the GF version needs label-checking)

---

### 🍖 Gluten-Free BBQ Sauce (salsa BBQ sin gluten)

**What is it?** Kansas City style BBQ sauce: thick, sweet, tomato-based, dark red-brown. You need 2 cups (500ml) for the chicken. If making the burnt ends backup, you need an additional 1.5 cups.

**⚠️ CRITICAL GF CHECK:** Many BBQ sauces contain malt vinegar (derived from barley — contains gluten) or wheat-based thickeners. Read the label.

**GF brands commonly available in Mexico:**
- **Sweet Baby Ray's Original** — GF ✅ (available at Chedraui Selecto and Walmart, imported)
- **Stubb's Original** — GF ✅ (available at Chedraui Selecto, imported)
- **Kraft Original** — check label, some formulations may vary in Mexico

**What to avoid:** Sauces that list "vinagre de malta" (malt vinegar), "almidón de trigo" (wheat starch), or "salsa de soya" (soy sauce, unless specified tamari/GF).

🟡 Difficulty: **Medium** (imported GF brands at Chedraui Selecto; verify label)

---

### 🧂 Seasoned Salt — Lawry's (sal sazonada)

**What is it?** Salt blended with sugar, paprika, turmeric, onion powder, and garlic powder. It's a reddish-orange fine powder that adds "restaurant" flavour to everything. Lawry's is the iconic brand.

**Where to find it:** Chedraui Selecto (international section). Walmart sometimes has it. Amazon Mexico carries it.

**Substitutions:** Make your own: 2 tbsp salt + 1 tsp sugar + 1/2 tsp paprika + 1/4 tsp turmeric + 1/4 tsp onion powder + 1/4 tsp garlic powder. Mix well.

🟡 Difficulty: **Medium** (imported product; DIY substitute is easy)

---

### 🌶️ Chili Powder — American Style (chile en polvo estilo americano)

**What is it?** NOT the same as Mexican "chile en polvo" (which is just pure ground dried chiles, like chile de árbol or ancho). American chili powder is a SPICE BLEND: ground dried chiles + cumin + oregano + garlic powder + sometimes salt. It's what Americans use to make "chili con carne."

**What does it taste like?** Mildly spicy, earthy, cumin-forward. Much milder than pure Mexican chile powder.

**Where to find it:** Chedraui Selecto (international section — McCormick "Chili Powder"). DAC Market. Amazon Mexico.

**Substitution (DIY):** 2 parts ground paprika + 1 part ground cumin + 1/2 part garlic powder + 1/2 part dried oregano + pinch of cayenne. Mix well.

🟡 Difficulty: **Medium** (imported blend; DIY from common Mexican spices)

---

### 🔴 Cayenne Pepper (pimienta de cayena / chile cayena en polvo)

**What is it?** Pure ground cayenne chile — very fine, bright orange-red powder. It's HOT. Used sparingly in the dry rubs and collard greens for background heat.

**Where to find it:** Most Mexican supermarkets carry this. Often labelled "pimienta de cayena" or "chile cayena molido." McCormick or local brands.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy** (common in Mexican spice aisles)

---

### 🟤 Brown Sugar — Dark and Light (azúcar morena oscura y clara)

**What is it?** White sugar with molasses added back in. Dark brown sugar has more molasses (stronger flavour, darker colour, more moisture). Light brown sugar has less. Both are soft and packable — when you squeeze it, it holds its shape.

**Dark brown sugar (azúcar morena oscura):** Used in the pulled pork (brine + dry rub). About 1.5 cups needed total.

**Light brown sugar (azúcar morena clara):** Used in the rib dry rub and the burnt ends backup. About 1/2 cup needed total.

**Where to find it:** The standard Mexican "azúcar morena" is midway between light and dark — it works fine for both. If you can only find one type, use it for everything. Chedraui Selecto carries both if you want the real distinction.

🟢 Difficulty: **Easy** (standard Mexican pantry item)

### TIP 1: How to Brine Pork Shoulder for Maximum Juiciness

**Source:** *Brine for Pork Shoulder Recipe*, Daily Cooking Recipe (4.9 stars, 9 ratings) — https://dailycookingrecipe.com/brine-for-pork-shoulder-recipe/

**What is brining?** Brining is soaking meat in a salt-sugar-water solution for 12–24 hours. The salt dissolves some of the meat proteins, allowing them to hold onto more water during cooking. The sugar helps with browning and balances the salt. The result: meat that stays juicy even after 12+ hours in the oven.

**Why brine?** Pork shoulder is a tough, working muscle with lots of connective tissue. Without brine, the outside can dry out before the inside breaks down. Brining seasons the meat all the way to the centre — not just on the surface.

**Step-by-step for THIS week's brine:**
1. **Tuesday afternoon/evening (the day before cooking):** In a large pot, combine 4 cups water, 4 cups apple cider, 1/2 cup kosher salt, 1/2 cup dark brown sugar. Heat gently and stir until completely dissolved — you should see NO salt grains floating. This is your brine base.
2. Remove from heat. Add 3 tbsp of the dry rub (already mixed), a pinch of red pepper flakes, and 2 bay leaves. Let it steep 5–10 minutes — this infuses the brine with spice flavour.
3. **CRITICAL: Cool the brine COMPLETELY before adding the pork.** Pouring warm brine over raw meat starts cooking it — you'll get tough, grey outer meat. Add ice cubes OR refrigerate the brine until completely cold.
4. Submerge the pork butt in the cold brine. It MUST be fully covered. Use a heavy plate on top to weigh it down if it floats. Cover with plastic wrap.
5. Refrigerate 12–24 hours. Minimum 12 hours for a 5 lb shoulder. Don't go past 24 hours or it gets too salty.
6. **Wednesday morning:** Remove pork from brine. Rinse BRIEFLY under cold water (removes surface salt). Pat COMPLETELY DRY with paper towels — wet meat won't form a good crust. Let it sit at room temperature 30 minutes before applying the dry rub and going into the oven.

**Key points from the experts (Kamado Joe Masterclass, https://kamadojoemasterclass.com/apple-cider-brined-pork-shoulder-for-pulled-pork/):**
- The salt and sugar MUST be fully dissolved. Undissolved grains = uneven brine.
- Cold brine = safe meat. Hot brine = partially cooked meat = tough.
- Pat completely dry after brining. Moisture on the surface = steam, not crust.
- Brining is NOT optional for this recipe. It's the step that transforms pork shoulder from "good pulled pork" to "the best pulled pork you've ever made."

### TIP 2: How to Remove the Membrane from Pork Ribs

**Source:** *How to Remove the Membrane From Pork Ribs*, The Spruce Eats (Danilo Alfaro, 800+ recipes published) — https://www.thespruceeats.com/remove-membrane-from-pork-ribs-995303

**What is the membrane?** On the bone side of a rack of pork ribs, there's a thin, translucent, silvery-white layer of tissue. It's called the peritoneum. When cooked, this membrane does NOT soften — it becomes tough, chewy, and plastic-like. It also blocks your dry rub from penetrating the meat. REMOVING IT IS THE SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT STEP FOR GOOD RIBS.

**How it feels:** Rubbery, slippery, strong — like a thin sheet of plastic or a balloon. When you pull it, it stretches. It's the only thing on the bone side that separates easily from the meat.

**Step-by-step — The Spruce Eats method:**
1. Place the rack of ribs **curved side down** (bone side up) on a cutting board. The white/silvery membrane is facing you.
2. Pat the bone side dry with a paper towel — grip matters.
3. At one end of the rack, slide the tip of a **butter knife** (not a sharp chef's knife — too dangerous) under the membrane. You want the dull edge, not the sharp edge. Work it between the membrane and the bone until you create a small gap.
4. Wiggle the knife side to side to enlarge the gap. When you have enough membrane lifted to grip it...
5. **Use a PAPER TOWEL for grip.** The membrane is incredibly slippery — your bare fingers will slide right off. Wrap a paper towel around your index finger and thumb, grab the loose edge of membrane, and pull FIRMLY.
6. Pull the membrane UP and AWAY from the ribs in one steady motion. Use your other hand to hold the rack steady. It should come off in one piece, like peeling a sticker.
7. If it tears mid-way (common), use the knife to start a new section and continue pulling.
8. Discard the membrane in the trash.

**What the pros say (Ollie George Cigliano, Private Chef, wikiHow — https://www.wikihow.life/Remove-Membrane-from-Ribs):**
- Start near the MIDDLE of the rack, not the edge — middle bones give you a better angle.
- The paper towel trick is the secret weapon. Without it, the membrane will slip every time.
- If you're struggling, needle-nose pliers work brilliantly — clean kitchen pliers, grip the membrane, pull steadily.
- Some pitmasters score the membrane (cut a crosshatch pattern) instead of removing it. But for oven-baked ribs, full removal gives better results — the rub actually reaches the meat.

### TIP 3: How to Baste BBQ Chicken Without Burning the Sugar

**Source:** *Two Ingredient Crispy Oven Baked BBQ Chicken*, Bowl of Delicious (4.93 stars, 1,095 ratings) — https://www.bowlofdelicious.com/two-ingredient-crispy-oven-baked-bbq-chicken/

Also: *Pro Tip: How to Use Sweet BBQ Sauces Without Burning Them*, Grill Masters Club — https://www.grillmastersclub.com/blogs/blog/pro-tip-how-to-use-sweet-bbq-sauces-without-burning-them

**The problem:** BBQ sauce is full of sugar (brown sugar, molasses, honey, ketchup). Sugar burns at high heat — it goes from caramelised (delicious, sticky, brown) to burnt (bitter, black, acrid) in about 60 seconds. Most people make the mistake of putting BBQ sauce on raw chicken and cooking it the entire time — that's how you get burnt, bitter skin.

**The solution — The Two-Temperature Method (from Bowl of Delicious):**
1. **FIRST PHASE — NO SAUCE: 400°F (200°C) for 25 minutes.** Season the chicken with dry rub only — no sauce. Place skin-side DOWN (yes, down — this renders fat from the skin, making it crispy). Bake 25 minutes. The chicken cooks, the skin crisps. No sauce = no burning.
2. **SECOND PHASE — SAUCE IN LAYERS: 425°F (218°C).** Remove chicken from oven. Turn oven UP to 425°F. Now start basting:
   - Baste the bottom side (currently facing up) with a thin layer of sauce.
   - Flip chicken so skin side is UP.
   - Baste skin side.
   - Return to oven for 7 minutes.
   - Remove, baste skin side again, return 7 minutes.
   - Repeat ONE MORE TIME (total: 3 basting cycles, 21 minutes at 425°F).
3. Each 7-minute cycle lets the previous sauce layer caramelise and tighten before the next layer goes on. By the third layer, you have a thick, glossy, sticky glaze — not burnt, not bitter.
4. **OPTIONAL BROIL:** If you want EXTRA char (those dark, crispy edges that are the best part), move the oven rack UP and broil for 3–5 minutes. **WATCH IT CONSTANTLY.** Sugar goes from perfect to burnt in seconds under the broiler. The moment you see dark char spots, pull it.

**The Grill Masters Club rules for sweet sauce:**
- Thin layers, not one thick coat. A thick layer burns before it sets.
- Sauce near the END, not at the beginning.
- "A little char is flavour. Burnt sugar is bitterness. The difference is heat control and timing."

### TIP 4: How to Make Collard Greens Tender (Not Tough or Bitter)

**Sources:**
- *Southern Collard Greens Recipe*, Butter Be Ready (5 stars, 80 ratings) — https://www.butterbeready.com/southern-collard-greens-recipe/
- *How to Clean Collard Greens*, Just Maika Cooking — https://www.justmaikacooking.com/cooks-notebook/how-to-clean-collard-greens-step-by-step/

**The secret:** Collard greens need TWO things to become tender: TIME and ACID. They're a tough, fibrous leaf — like kale but even heartier — and they will NOT soften in 10 minutes like spinach. You must cook them low and slow (at least 1 hour) with an acidic liquid (vinegar + broth).

**Step 1: WASH THEM PROPERLY — DO NOT SKIP THIS.**
Collard greens grow in sandy soil. The leaves have deep folds and crevices where grit hides. A quick rinse under the tap is NOT enough — you WILL taste sand in your food.

The sink method (from Just Maika Cooking):
1. Clean your kitchen sink thoroughly with soap, rinse completely.
2. Fill halfway with COLD water. (Hot water wilts the leaves.)
3. Submerge all the greens. Swish them around with your hands for about 30 seconds — push them down, move them side to side. The greens float, so you need to keep pushing them under.
4. Let them soak 5–10 minutes. The dirt settles to the bottom.
5. LIFT the greens out (don't drain the sink with the greens in it — you'll just pour the grit back over them).
6. Drain the sink, clean it, and repeat the soak-and-swish 2–3 more times until the water stays clear.
7. Pat leaves dry before cutting.

**Step 2: REMOVE THE STEMS.**
The thick central stem on each leaf is tough, fibrous, and bitter. It will NEVER soften no matter how long you cook it. Fold each leaf in half along the stem, and cut or tear the stem out. The small, young, tender leaves near the centre of the bunch don't need stemming.

**Step 3: CUT INTO STRIPS.**
Stack a few leaves, roll them into a cigar shape, and slice across into thin ribbons (about 1 cm / 1/2 inch wide). The thinner the strips, the faster they cook.

**Step 4: BUILD THE "POT LIKKER" FIRST.**
"Pot likker" (or "pot liquor") is the Southern term for the cooking liquid. It's the flavour foundation. In a large pot, sauté the chopped onion in the reserved bacon fat until golden. Add smashed garlic. Then add the chicken broth, apple cider vinegar, Tabasco, sugar, seasoned salt, and pepper. Bring to a boil. THIS liquid is what makes the greens taste good — it must be well-seasoned before the greens go in. Taste it — it should be tangy, slightly sweet, salty, and spicy.

**Step 5: ADD THE GREENS IN BATCHES.**
20 cups of raw collard greens will NOT fit in the pot all at once. Add a big handful, cover and let it wilt for 2 minutes, stir, then add more. Keep going until all the greens are in. They will reduce to about half the pot volume.

**Step 6: LOW AND SLOW — AT LEAST 1 HOUR.**
Reduce heat to a gentle simmer. Cover. Cook at least 1 hour, stirring occasionally (move greens from the bottom to the top so they cook evenly). The greens should be tender and the liquid should be mostly absorbed. If they're still tough after 1 hour, cook another 15–30 minutes — they'll get there.

**What the experts say about tenderness (Butter Be Ready):**
- "True Southern collard greens are cooked low and slow. Once the liquor has been made, the greens are stuffed into the pot and cooked on low. Do not be afraid to over-stuff the pot. Greens cook down to practically nothing."
- The vinegar is ESSENTIAL — it cuts through the richness and brightens the earthy greens. Don't skip it.
- The greens actually taste BETTER the next day — flavours meld overnight.

### TIP 5: How to Avoid Gluey Mashed Potatoes

**Source:** J. Kenji López-Alt, *Pommes Purée (Rich and Creamy Mashed Potatoes)*, Serious Eats — https://www.seriouseats.com/rich-and-creamy-mashed-potatoes-recipe

Also: *The Best Way To Avoid Gluey Mashed Potatoes*, Mashed (citing Serious Eats and The Kitchn) — https://www.mashed.com/636326/the-best-way-to-avoid-gluey-mashed-potatoes/

**Why do mashed potatoes get gluey?** Potatoes are about 85% starch. When you overwork cooked potatoes — with a blender, food processor, electric mixer, or even aggressive hand-mashing — you rupture the starch granules. The released starch combines with water and turns into a sticky, gluey paste. Think "wallpaper paste" texture. It's the most common mashed potato disaster and **it cannot be fixed** once it happens.

**The irreversible rule:** Kenji López-Alt and The Kitchn both confirm: gluey mashed potatoes cannot be saved. You can't remove excess starch once it's been incorporated. The only options are to (a) start over with a fresh batch, or (b) transform them into something else (potato croquettes, shepherd's pie topping, add cheese and bake as a gratin).

**How to avoid it — 5 rules:**

1. **HAND MASH ONLY.** Use a simple potato masher — the old-fashioned handheld tool with a grid of squares. Do NOT use: blender, food processor, stick/immersion blender, or electric hand mixer. Any of these will overwork the potatoes in seconds. A potato masher gently breaks the potatoes apart without shearing the starch granules.

2. **Start with COLD water.** Put the cubed potatoes in the pot, then fill with cold water to cover by at least 10 cm / 4 inches. Starting in cold water (not boiling) helps the potatoes cook evenly from edge to centre. If you drop them into already-boiling water, the outside overcooks and turns mealy before the centre is done.

3. **Cook just until tender — no more.** Test with a fork or paring knife: it should slide in with NO resistance. The potato cube should fall apart when you jab it. This takes about 15 minutes from the time the water reaches a boil. Overcooked potatoes absorb too much water and turn watery and gluey when mashed.

4. **Drain and DRY.** After draining in a colander, return the potatoes to the hot pot (heat OFF). Leave them for 1–2 minutes, shaking the pot occasionally. This evaporates excess surface moisture. Wet potatoes = watery mash.

5. **Add butter FIRST, milk second, and fold gently.** Put the butter into the hot, dry potatoes and mash gently until the butter is melted and distributed. Then add the warm milk in small additions, folding with a spatula or wooden spoon — not beating. WARM milk (not cold) prevents the potatoes from cooling down and seizing up.

**What Serious Eats says about potato variety:**
Russet/Idaho potatoes are HIGH starch, LOW moisture — they make the fluffiest mash. Yukon Gold potatoes are MEDIUM starch — they make a creamier, denser mash that is harder to overwork into glue. For fluffy American-style mashed potatoes, russets are ideal.

**Rinsing trick (from Serious Eats, via Mashed):** Rinse the cubed potatoes under cold water before cooking. This washes away surface starch released during cutting — fewer free starch granules means less chance of gluey mash.

### TIP 6: How to Know When Baked Beans Are Done

**Source:** RecipeTin Eats, *Homemade Baked Beans (Southern Style)* (4.93 stars, 55 ratings) — https://www.recipetineats.com/homemade-baked-beans-bacon-southern-style/

Also: *Cooking Dried Beans*, Simon & Schuster — https://www.simonandschuster.com/c/cooking-dried-beans

**Using canned beans (like we are):** Canned kidney beans are already fully cooked. You're not cooking the beans — you're simmering them in the sauce to absorb flavour. The key is to stop BEFORE the beans break down and turn the whole pot into mush.

**Signs the baked beans are done:**
1. **The sauce** should be thick and glossy, coating the back of a spoon. If it's still watery and thin at 60 minutes, cook uncovered for another 10–15 minutes to reduce.
2. **The beans** should hold their kidney shape but be tender when bitten. A few may have split skins — that's normal. If MOST have split and the sauce is bean-coloured (the beans are disintegrating), you've gone too far.
3. **A spoon dragged through the pot** should leave a trail that slowly fills in — not instantly (too thin) and not at all (too thick/dry).

**The simmering rule from Simon & Schuster's bean guide:**
"Simmer the beans until they're done. This is vague, but there are many variables. Start with 30 minutes on the timer, then start tasting. Check every 10–15 minutes until the beans are tender but still firm — they shouldn't be falling apart."

**For our recipe:** 1 hour at a gentle simmer, covered, stirring occasionally. At 45 minutes, taste a bean. If it's tender and the sauce is thick, you're done. If the sauce is thin, uncover and cook 10–15 minutes more. If the sauce is TOO thick and the beans are sticking, add a splash of water.

### TIP 7: How to Rest Meat Before Shredding/Slicing

**Applies to:** Pulled pork, ribs, BBQ chicken.

**Why rest?** When meat cooks, the muscle fibres contract and squeeze moisture toward the centre. Cutting or shredding immediately lets that moisture pour out onto the cutting board. Resting allows the fibres to relax and reabsorb some of those juices.

**Resting times:**
- Pulled pork: Rest AT LEAST 1 hour, covered loosely with foil. The recipe says to turn off the oven and leave the pork inside for 1–2 hours — this is a gentle, gradual rest that keeps the meat warm while it relaxes. Perfect.
- Ribs: Rest 5–10 minutes after the vinegar dip before cutting into individual ribs.
- BBQ Chicken: Rest 5 minutes after the final basting/broil before serving.

**The Mountain Kitchen (https://www.themountainkitchen.com/brined-smoked-pork-shoulder/) says:** "At 200°F, the pork is about to fall apart. Allow the meat to rest for at least 1 hour under loosely tented foil. Allowing the pork to rest before slicing or chopping lets the meat fibers relax. Some of the dissolved proteins redistribute the moisture that was driven out and reabsorbed."
