### Tamari (Gluten-Free Soy Sauce)

**What it is:** Japanese-style soy sauce made mostly from soybeans, **without wheat**. Regular Chinese/Japanese soy sauce is brewed with wheat and is **not** gluten-free. Tamari is the #1 ingredient that makes this entire week safe for the customer.

**What it looks like:** Dark brown liquid in a glass bottle, often labeled “Tamari” and “Gluten Free.” Common brands: **Kikkoman Gluten-Free Tamari**, **San-J Tamari**.

<img align="right" width="100" src="https://kikkomanusa.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/00808-GF-Tamari-Soy-Sauce.webp">

**What it tastes like:** Salty, deep, savory (umami) — like soy sauce but a bit richer and slightly less sharp.

**Where to buy in Playa del Carmen:**
- **Walmart** (30 Av. y Calle 8): International / organic aisle — look for Kikkoman GF
- **Chedraui Selecto** (Plaza Las Américas): Better international selection
- **DAC Market** (30 Av. entre Calle 14 y 14 Bis): Often has Asian sauces
- **Amazon Mexico:** “tamari sin gluten” or “Kikkoman tamari”

**Difficulty:** 🟡 medium

**If you can't find it:** Do **not** use regular salsa soya. In a true emergency, coconut aminos + extra salt can work but the flavor is sweeter/different. Maggi Jugo is sometimes GF — **read the label** every time (formulas change).

**⚠️ Critical:** Buy the largest bottle you can. Every single dish this week uses tamari.

### GF Dark Soy Sauce (homemade — cook it, don’t just stir)

**What it is:** Real Chinese dark soy (*lǎo chōu*) is thicker, darker, and slightly sweeter than regular/light soy. It gives braises their mahogany color. Almost all bottled dark soy contains **wheat**. In Playa you make a GF version at home.

**Important:** This is **not** “dump molasses into cold tamari and pray.” Better results come from a **short simmer** so the sweetener dissolves, the sauce thickens slightly, and it looks glossy like real dark soy.

**Source:** Base ratio from [The Woks of Life dark soy substitute](https://thewoksoflife.com/dark-soy-sauce/) (2 tsp soy + ½ tsp molasses + ⅛ tsp sugar per Tbsp). Batch is simmered 3–5 min (common homemade method). Full recipe is at the top of `weekly-menu.md` / `menu-semanal-espanol.md`.

**Batch (½ cup / whole week):**
- 80 ml tamari + 20 ml molasses + 1 tsp sugar + 1 Tbsp water  
- Small pan → medium heat until dissolved → gentle simmer **3–5 min**, stirring → cool → jar in fridge (1 month)

**What molasses looks like:** Thick, almost black syrup (melaza). Piloncillo (Mexican cane sugar cone) works if melted dark.

**Difficulty:** 🟢 easy (10 minutes, once per week)

**Where for ingredients:** Melaza/piloncillo — any Chedraui / Soriana / Walmart. Tamari — see above.

**Emergency mid-cook:** cold-stir 2 tsp tamari + ½ tsp molasses + ⅛ tsp sugar (Woks of Life quick mix). Prefer the simmered jar.

### Dry Sherry (Jerez Seco) — instead of Shaoxing Wine

**What it is:** Shaoxing wine is the classic Chinese cooking wine, but **almost every bottle contains wheat**. Dry sherry (jerez seco) is the standard gluten-free substitute recommended by Omnivore’s Cookbook and many GF Chinese cooks.

**What it looks like:** Amber wine in a bottle, sold in the wine section. Look for “jerez seco” or “dry sherry” — **not** sweet cream sherry, and prefer real drinking sherry over “cooking wine” loaded with salt.

<img align="right" width="100" src="https://tasteohiowines.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/Dry-Sherry.webp">

**What it tastes like:** Nutty, dry, slightly oxidized wine aroma. When cooked, the alcohol burns off and leaves depth.

**Where to buy:**
- **Walmart / Chedraui / Soriana:** Wine aisle — jerez
- Any liquor store in Playa

**Difficulty:** 🟢 easy

**If you can't find it:** Dry white wine + tiny pinch of sugar is a distant backup. Do **not** buy Shaoxing unless the label explicitly says gluten-free (very rare; most say “contains wheat”).

### Star Anise (Anís Estrellado)

**What it is:** A hard, star-shaped spice from a Chinese tree. One of the “warm” spices in red-braised meat. Not the same as anise seed or fennel.

**What it looks like:** A rusty-brown star with 6–8 points, about the size of a coin. Sold whole in jars or bags.

<img align="right" width="100" src="https://images-na.ssl-images-amazon.com/images/I/61olt9huZUL.jpg">

**What it tastes like:** Strong licorice / sweet spice. A little goes a long way. You simmer it whole and usually remove it before serving.

**Where to buy:**
- **DAC Market:** Best bet for bulk
- **Walmart / Chedraui:** Spice aisle — “anís estrellado”
- Amazon Mexico: “anís estrellado entero”

**Difficulty:** 🟡 medium

**If you can't find it:** Skip it — the dish still works with cinnamon + ginger. Do not substitute star anise candy or anise extract.

### A) TECHNIQUE SCHOOL — definitions + how to do them well

---

#### 1. Braising (estofar / guisar a fuego lento)

**Definition:** Braising means cooking food (usually tougher meat) **slowly in a covered pot with a small-to-medium amount of liquid**, at a gentle simmer, until the meat becomes tender and the liquid becomes a flavorful sauce. It is *not* deep-frying and *not* boiling in a huge pot of water like pasta.

Think of it as: **sear or start the flavor → add liquid halfway up the food → low heat + lid → wait patiently → reduce sauce at the end if needed.**

**This week you braise:** Hong Shao Rou (pork) and Chinese Beef Stew. Three Cup Chicken is a **short braise / simmer-then-reduce**.

**Why it works:** Tough cuts (pork shoulder, beef chuck) have collagen (the chewy connective tissue). Long, moist, gentle heat turns collagen into gelatin → meat becomes soft and the sauce feels rich and silky.

**How to braise effectively — rules that matter:**

| Rule | What to do | Why |
|------|------------|-----|
| Right cut | Use shoulder, chuck, shank — not super-lean steak | Lean meat dries out; collagen-rich meat becomes tender |
| Liquid level | Liquid should come about **½ to ¾ of the way up** the meat — not drowning it, not dry | Too much liquid = boiled meat, weak sauce. Too little = burns |
| Heat level | After the boil, keep a **gentle simmer** — small bubbles, not a hard rolling boil | Hard boil toughens meat and can burn the bottom |
| Lid | Cover for most of the time; leave a small gap if the recipe says so | Trap steam to cook through; gap lets sauce slowly concentrate |
| Don’t rush | Pork ~1½ hr; beef chuck ~2+ hr after blanch | Undercooked braise = chewy. Time is the ingredient |
| Stir occasionally | Every 15–20 min for long braises | Prevents sticking/burning on the bottom |
| Finish | If sauce is thin when meat is done: uncover, raise heat, reduce | Sauce should coat a spoon or cling to meat |
| Rest | Braises taste better next day | Flavors marry in the fridge |

**How to know the meat is done:**
- A fork slides in with little resistance
- A cube should pull apart with two forks, not bounce back like rubber
- If still tough: **more time, not higher heat**. Add a splash of water if dry, cover, keep simmering 20–30 min more, check again

**Common braising mistakes:**
1. Boiling hard the whole time → tough, dry edges
2. Opening the lid every 2 minutes → heat escapes, takes forever
3. Adding all the salt/soy at the very start (for some braises) → slows tenderizing (see beef stew tip below)
4. Walking away for 2 hours with high heat → burnt bottom, bitter sauce
5. Stopping when meat is done but sauce is still watery → bland plate over rice

**Simple heat picture:**
- **High:** boil to start, or reduce sauce at the end
- **Medium-low / low:** the long braise itself  
  If you hear aggressive splattering or see huge bubbles, turn it down.

Sources: Omnivore’s Cookbook braising recipes (Hong Shao Rou, Chinese Beef Stew); The Woks of Life red-cooked pork method; general Chinese red-braise practice (hóng shāo).

---

#### 2. Blanching meat (blanquear / 焯水 chāo shuǐ)

**Definition:** Briefly boiling raw meat in water, skimming the foam that rises, then draining (and sometimes rinsing) before the real cooking starts.

**This week:** Start of Hong Shao Rou and Chinese Beef Stew.

**What the foam is:** Mostly coagulated blood proteins and scum — not “dirt,” but it makes sauce cloudy and can taste off if left in.

**How to blanch well:**
1. Put meat in the pot **first**, then cover with **cold** water (starting cold helps draw scum out evenly)
2. Bring to a boil over medium-high
3. When foam appears, skim it off with a spoon or fine strainer — keep skimming until the surface is clearer
4. Boil the time the recipe says (usually 1–15 min depending on dish)
5. Drain. For Hong Shao Rou: **save the liquid** to braise with. For some recipes you discard it — follow the recipe card
6. Rinse meat under cold water if the recipe says so; **pat very dry** before caramel or searing

**Tip:** Don’t walk away during the first boil — foam rises fast and can overflow.

Sources: Omnivore’s Cookbook Hong Shao Rou blanching step; East Asian braise prep practice.

---

#### 3. Caramelizing sugar in oil (炒糖色 chǎo táng sè)

**Definition:** Melting sugar in a little oil until it turns light amber, then coating meat in that caramel. This is what gives Hong Shao Rou its shiny red-brown color and deep sweet-savory base — **before** the long braise.

**This is the scariest-looking step for beginners. It is easy if you go slow.**

**Rules (Caroline’s Cooking + Omnivore’s Cookbook + HSR guides):**
1. Pot must be **bone-dry** — any water makes oil/sugar explode and spit
2. Meat must be **patted dry with paper towels**
3. Low heat. Add sugar + oil
4. **Swirl the pot** to move sugar — **do not stir with a wet spoon** (can crystallize into hard white clumps)
5. Watch without leaving the stove. Color goes: clear → pale gold → **light amber** ← STOP HERE
6. If it goes dark brown/black: it is burnt and bitter. Dump it, wash pot, restart. You cannot fix burnt caramel
7. Carefully add meat (it may sputter). Turn to coat. Then add wine/liquid to stop the cooking (deglaze)

**Color guide:** Think the color of light honey or a new copper penny — not coffee, not chocolate.

---

#### 4. Reducing a sauce (reducir)

**Definition:** Boiling a sauce **uncovered** so water evaporates and the sauce gets thicker, glossier, and more concentrated in flavor.

**This week:** End of Hong Shao Rou, end of Three Cup Chicken, and anytime a braise is tender but still watery.

**How:**
1. Meat is already cooked through
2. Remove the lid
3. Raise heat to medium or medium-high
4. Stir often (sugar + soy can burn)
5. Stop when sauce **coats the back of a spoon** or **clings to the meat** instead of running like water

**Too far?** If it gets as thick as tar or starts smelling scorched, add a splash of water and turn heat down.

Sources: The Woks of Life Three Cup Chicken (“rapidly reduce until it clings”); Omnivore’s Cookbook HSR reduction step.

---

#### 5. Simmer vs boil (hervir suave vs hervor fuerte)

**Boil:** Big rolling bubbles, surface moving hard. Good for: starting a blanch, reducing sauce fast, cooking pasta.  
**Simmer:** Small gentle bubbles around the edges, surface barely trembling. Good for: **all long braises**.

If your braise is boiling hard for an hour, turn the knob down. Gentle heat + time = tender. Violent heat + time = tough outside, burnt pot.

---

#### 6. Searing / browning (sellar / dorar)

**Definition:** Cooking the surface of meat or vegetables over fairly high heat until it browns. Brown = flavor (Maillard reaction). Gray steamed meat = less flavor.

**This week:** Three Cup Chicken (brown the chicken first); eggplant (char the pieces); green beans (blister).

**Rules:**
- Pan should be hot before food goes in
- **Don’t crowd the pan** — if pieces touch too much, they steam instead of brown. Cook in batches
- Don’t move the food every second — let it sit 1–2 minutes to develop color, then turn
- Dry surface browns better than wet surface (pat dry)

---

#### 7. Stir-frying (saltear)

**Definition:** Cooking small pieces of food quickly in a hot pan/wok with a little oil, tossing often. Total cook time is often 5–15 minutes once the pan is hot.

**This week:** Final minutes of eggplant (with sauce), final minutes of green beans (with pork and seasoning).

**Golden rule of stir-fry:** **Everything must be cut and measured BEFORE the pan gets hot.** Once you start, you will not have time to mince garlic.

That prep-before-heat habit is called **mise en place** (French: “everything in its place”). Woks of Life repeats this on almost every stir-fry recipe: stove time is short; prep is the real work.

**Stir-fry order (typical):**
1. Heat pan → oil
2. Aromatics (garlic, ginger, chile) — seconds only, don’t burn
3. Main ingredient (or return pre-cooked veg)
4. Pre-mixed sauce
5. Toss until coated → off heat

---

#### 8. Dry-frying / dry-searing vegetables (干煸 gān biān)

**Definition:** Cooking vegetables (often green beans) in a pan with little or no sauce at first, long enough that they wrinkle, blister, and pick up smoky charred spots. Seasoning is added **at the end**.

**This week:** Sichuan dry-fried green beans.

**What “done” looks like:** Beans are wrinkled, with brown blister spots, tender but not mushy — **not** bright shiny boiled green.

**Patience is the technique.** 6–10+ minutes of flipping. If you rush and sauce early, you get steamed beans in soy water.

Sources: Omnivore’s Cookbook dry-fried green beans; The Woks of Life Sichuan dry-fried green beans.

---

#### 9. Deglazing (desglasar)

**Definition:** After browning meat or caramelizing sugar, you pour in wine or stock and scrape the brown bits stuck to the bottom of the pot. Those brown bits are pure flavor — you want them in the sauce, not burned onto the pan.

**This week:** After caramel + pork in Hong Shao Rou — add dry sherry and scrape with a wooden spoon.

---

#### 10. Thickening with cornstarch (maicena)

**Definition:** Mixing cornstarch with cold water (or cold sauce liquids) then adding to a hot pan so the sauce turns glossy and thick in seconds.

**Rules:**
- Always mix cornstarch into **cold** liquid first (slurry). Dumping dry cornstarch into hot liquid makes lumps
- Stir the slurry again right before pouring (it settles)
- Sauce thickens fast — toss immediately
- If too thick: splash of water. If too thin: a little more slurry

**This week:** Eggplant garlic sauce.

---

#### 11. Heat control — the beginner’s best skill

You do not need fancy equipment. You need to **look and listen**:

| What you see/hear | What it usually means | What to do |
|-------------------|----------------------|------------|
| Huge angry bubbles, lots of steam, rattling lid | Too hot for braising | Turn down to low/medium-low |
| No bubbles at all, nothing moving | Too cold — not cooking | Turn up slightly until gentle bubbles |
| Smoke pouring from oil (not a little wisp) | Pan too hot / oil burning | Off heat a moment, lower, restart |
| Garlic turns dark brown in 5 seconds | Aromatics burning | Lower heat; next time add aromatics to cooler oil or for less time |
| Sauce smells sweet then suddenly acrid | Sugar scorching during reduce | Stir, lower heat, add splash of water |

**When in doubt for a braise: lower is safer than higher.** Extra 20 minutes at low heat fixes tough meat. Burnt pot does not un-burn.

---

#### 12. Tasting as you cook

Professional cooks taste. You should too.

- Taste the braising liquid halfway (carefully — hot!)
- Taste again before packing
- Adjust: more tamari if flat/salty-needed; pinch of sugar if harsh; splash of water if too strong; salt only at the end if needed

Your tongue is the final recipe step.

---

#### 13. Food safety basics for batch cooking

- Cool food before sealing tight lids when possible (or leave lids cracked until steam stops) so condensation doesn’t make rice mushy
- Fridge: cooked food 3–5 days max for this week’s dishes
- Freezer: label with **dish name + date**
- Reheat until steaming hot all the way through
- Thaw freezer meals in the fridge overnight — not on the counter all day
- Use clean spoons for tasting; don’t double-dip from the tasting spoon into the pot after it touched your mouth

### B) THIS WEEK’S DISH TIPS (techniques applied)

---

#### Hong Shao Rou — braise + caramel

1. Blanch → dry meat well  
2. Caramel sugar to light amber only  
3. Deglaze with jerez, add liquid, **gentle simmer 1½ hr**  
4. Reduce uncovered until glossy  
5. If meat is still tough at 1½ hr: keep simmering, don’t crank heat to max  

**Salt note:** Tamari is already salty — taste before adding more salt.

---

#### Chinese Beef Stew — long braise

1. Blanch and skim well (cleaner broth)  
2. Spices + wine first; simmer  
3. **Add tamari/salt after the first ~40 min** — Maggie Zhu notes salt early slows tenderizing  
4. Potatoes and carrots go in **near the end** so they don’t turn to mush  
5. Done when beef yields to a fork; sauce stays a bit brothy (good over rice)  

Source: Omnivore’s Cookbook Chinese Beef Stew method notes.

---

#### Three Cup Chicken — short braise + hard reduce

1. Infuse oil with ginger/garlic/sesame (don’t burn garlic)  
2. Sear chicken until golden  
3. Add liquids, cover, simmer ~15–18 min  
4. **Uncover and reduce hard** until sauce clings — this is the whole point of the dish  
5. Scallions or Thai basil only at the very end  

Source: The Woks of Life Three Cup Chicken.

---

#### Garlic Eggplant — soak, sear, last-second sauce

1. Salt-water soak 15 min → drain → **pat very dry**  
2. Light cornstarch coat  
3. Sear in a **single layer** (batches!) until soft and charred  
4. Garlic 20–30 seconds only  
5. Pre-mixed sauce in last — thickens in seconds; toss fast  

Oven assist for big batches: 220°C sheet pans, then finish with sauce in a hot pan.

Source: Omnivore’s Cookbook Chinese Eggplant with Garlic Sauce.

---

#### Dry-fried green beans — blister first, season last

1. Beans must be dry  
2. Blister/char until wrinkled (patience)  
3. Brown pork + aromatics  
4. Return beans; add wine/tamari/sugar; high heat 1–2 min to coat  
5. Should taste savory and smoky, not watery  

Sources: Omnivore’s Cookbook + The Woks of Life dry-fried green beans.

---

#### Jasmine rice — absorption method

1. Rinse until water is mostly clear (removes extra starch → fluffier rice)  
2. Correct water ratio; tight lid; low heat  
3. **Do not lift the lid** during the 15 min cook  
4. Rest 10 min off heat still covered, then fluff  
5. Cool slightly before packing  

If rice is wet/mushy: too much water or lid lifted. If hard in the center: a bit more water next time, or sprinkle water and steam 5 more min.

### D) Encouragement (read this when something goes wrong)

Cooking is a skill built by reps, not by talent. If the caramel burns once, you learned the color line. If the beef needs 30 extra minutes, that is normal — every piece of meat is a little different. If the sauce is thin, you reduce. If it’s thick, you loosen with water.

This week’s braises are **forgiving**. Time and low heat fix most problems. You are not expected to be perfect on the first Chinese batch — you are expected to follow the steps, taste, and adjust.

You’ve got this.
