#### SPICES & SEASONINGS

**🟡 Garam Masala** (need 3 tbsp)
What it is: A blend of ground spices — cinnamon, cardamom, clove, cumin, coriander, black pepper, nutmeg. The core flavour of Indian cooking. Without it, the food won't taste Indian.

Where to buy:
1. **Walmart** — OG2 Especias Garam Masala (confirmed on walmart.com.mx). Look in spice aisle, possibly "Importados" or "Gourmet."
2. **Chedraui** — check imported/gourmet section.
3. **Amazon Mexico** — Rani Garam Masala (~$120-180 MXN) or Badia Garam Masala (~$80 MXN).
4. **DAC Market** — might carry it in bulk. Ask.

If you can't find it, MAKE IT YOURSELF: Mix in a jar — 1 tbsp ground cumin + 1½ tsp each ground coriander, ground cardamom, ground black pepper + 1 tsp ground cinnamon + ½ tsp each ground clove, ground nutmeg. Makes ~4 tbsp.

---

**🟢 Ground Cumin** (need 4 tbsp) — Regular cumin. Any supermarket. Walmart: La Guacamaya 50g ~$15 MXN.

**🟢 Ground Coriander** (need 4 tbsp) — Ground coriander seeds, NOT fresh cilantro. Tastes citrusy and warm. Any supermarket. Whole seeds are easier to find — grind them yourself.

**🟢 Turmeric Powder** (need 3 tbsp) — Intense yellow spice. Stains everything — wash hands and cutting board immediately. Any supermarket or DAC Market in bulk.

**🟢 Paprika / Sweet Pimentón** (need 3 tbsp) — Ground dried bell pepper. NOT spicy. Walmart: Carmencita 75g ~$33 MXN.

**🟢 Chili Powder / Cayenne** (need 1½ tbsp) — Pure ground chili. NOT American "chili powder" blend. Any supermarket: "Chile en Polvo" or "Cayena Molida."

**🟢 Ground Cinnamon** — Any supermarket. Walmart: Great Value 50g ~$40 MXN.

**🟡 Cinnamon Sticks** (need 2) — Any supermarket spice section. DAC Market. Very common in Mexico.

**🔴 Green Cardamom Pods** (need 6) — Small green pods with black seeds inside, smell floral-sweet. DAC Market is best bet. Also Amazon. Not confirmed at Walmart/Chedraui. If unavailable: skip whole pods but ensure cardamom powder is in your garam masala.

**🟡 Whole Cloves** (need 6) — Common in Mexico (ponche, stews). Any supermarket.

**🟢 Ground Nutmeg** (pinch) — Any supermarket.

**🟡 Whole Cumin Seeds** (need 1½ tsp) — DAC Market (bulk) or jarred at Walmart/Chedraui.

**🟡 Whole Coriander Seeds** (need 3 tsp) — DAC Market (bulk). Also at Walmart/Chedraui: "Semillas de Cilantro."

**🔴 Mustard Seeds** (need ¾ tsp) — Tiny black/brown seeds that pop when heated. DAC Market bulk spices. Unlikely at supermarkets. If unavailable: skip — recipe works without.

**🔴 Kasoori Methi / Dried Fenugreek Leaves** (need 2 tbsp) — Smells like sweet hay and curry. Gives that "Indian restaurant" flavour. Amazon Mexico: Rani brand 28g (~$90-150 MXN). DAC Market — ask. If unavailable: skip in both recipes. Do NOT use epazote.

**🟡 Tamarind Concentrate** (cooking paste, NOT drink mix) — need 3 tsp. Dark brown, sticky, sour. Walmart Asian/international section — check label. Chedraui international. DAC Market very likely. If unavailable: substitute 1 tbsp lime juice + ½ tsp brown sugar per tsp tamarind.

**🟡 Asafoetida / Hing** (OPTIONAL, pinch) — ⚠️ Most brands contain wheat flour. SKIP ENTIRELY this week. Fresh garlic/onion replaces it.

## Part 2: Cooking Tips

> **Sources:** These tips are drawn from interviews with Indian chefs and food writers published by Mashed (Jan 2024), NPR's Kitchen Window column by Monica Bhide (Dec 2011), and the Indian cooking site Tea for Turmeric.

---

### Tip 1: Always Bloom Your Spices (It's Not Optional)

"Not all but most spices need to be bloomed in hot oil to release their flavor. If you don't do this, you'll end up with a weaker flavor profile and also the taste of raw spices — raw turmeric in particular is very strong and overpowering."

— Chef Deepak Saxena, Desi PDX / Masala Lab, in [Mashed](https://www.mashed.com/1495300/mistakes-everyone-makes-cooking-indian-food/) (Jan 2024)

**What to do:** Heat 2 tbsp oil or ghee over medium-low heat. Add your spices and cook until fragrant — usually 1-2 minutes. Don't skip this step. For this week's recipes, this happens when you fry the spice paste in the Goan fish curry, when you toast the ground spices in the Rogan Josh, and during the tempering step (tadka) of the Dal Makhani.

**How to test oil temperature:** "Drop a single cumin seed or mustard seed into the oil. If it sizzles and crackles, the oil is ready."

— Shilpa Joshi, food blogger at Some Indian Girl, in [Mashed](https://www.mashed.com/1495300/mistakes-everyone-makes-cooking-indian-food/) (Jan 2024)

---

### Tip 2: Master the Tadka (Tempering) — The Secret to Indian Aroma

"Hot fat has an amazing ability to extract and retain the essence, aroma and flavor of spices and herbs and then carry this essence with it when it is added to a dish."

— Monica Bhide, in [NPR Kitchen Window](https://www.npr.org/2011/12/07/143251451/the-crackling-spices-of-indian-tempering) (Dec 2011)

Tadka (tempering) is when you heat whole spices in hot oil or ghee and then add that infused fat to your dish. For this week, you'll do a tadka for the Dal Makhani (cumin seeds in hot oil, then ginger, garlic, chili).

**Critical rules from NPR's article:**
- **No water is ever added to a tadka.** The fat must be pure oil or ghee.
- **The ghee or oil should be very hot at first, then reduce to medium** once spices are added.
- **Order matters:** "Those requiring longer cooking added earlier and those requiring less cooking added later." Whole cumin seeds go first; chopped garlic goes last (burns fastest).
- **It happens fast** — "The crackling of the spices or change in their color indicates that the process is complete. This usually takes only seconds."
- **You cannot save a burned tadka.** "Your nose will tell you when you've burned it; it is not a smell you will easily forget." Start over.
- **Have everything ready before you start.** "Have your mise en place — as there will be no time once the tempering process has started."

---

### Tip 3: Slow Cooking = Flavour (Don't Rush)

"Not being mindful of stove temperature or trying to up the heat to rush through the cooking process can affect the development of flavors. Allow the spices to release their flavors gradually, and let the dish simmer to develop complexity."

— Shilpa Joshi, in [Mashed](https://www.mashed.com/1495300/mistakes-everyone-makes-cooking-indian-food/) (Jan 2024)

"A lot of Indian food is cooked by using a slow cooking method, so you need to have a lot of patience."

— Chef Balpreet Singh Chadha, Sanjh, in [Mashed](https://www.mashed.com/1495300/mistakes-everyone-makes-cooking-indian-food/) (Jan 2024)

**This week's application:** The Lamb Rogan Josh simmers for 1 hour 45 minutes. The Dal Makhani simmers 30 minutes after adding legumes. Do not crank the heat to speed these up. The long simmer is what develops depth.

---

### Tip 4: Prep Everything Before You Start (Mise en Place)

"Cooking Indian food does require you to have your mise en place game be strong as it's fairly prep heavy. This way, while you are cooking and using techniques you are not used to — like making a tadka — you don't need to keep stepping away and possibly burn your spices."

— Chef Deepak Saxena and Shilpa Joshi, in [Mashed](https://www.mashed.com/1495300/mistakes-everyone-makes-cooking-indian-food/) (Jan 2024)

**What to do:** Measure all spices into small bowls BEFORE turning on the stove. Chop all onions, garlic, ginger. Open all cans. Have everything at arm's reach. This is especially critical for the Goan fish curry (spice paste), the dal tadka, and the Rogan Josh (many spices added in rapid succession).

---

### Tip 5: Yogurt Marinade = Tender Chicken (Do It Right)

From [Indian Healthy Recipes](https://www.indianhealthyrecipes.com/butter-chicken/) and [Drink-Milk.com](https://www.drink-milk.com/easy-butter-chicken/):

**Why it works:** "Greek yogurt and yogurt in general, contain both calcium and lactic acid. Together they are able to break down the proteins in chicken and meat leaving you with a tender and moist result." The lactic acid makes yogurt-based marinades **gentler than acid-based marinades** (like citrus or vinegar), which can make meat tough or mushy if overused.

**Key rules for this week's Butter Chicken:**
- **Use thick yogurt** (Greek or hung curd). Regular thin yogurt releases too much moisture during cooking, washing the marinade off and leaving bland chicken.
- **Pat chicken dry** before marinating so the yogurt sticks.
- **Marinate overnight if possible** (minimum 3 hours). The longer, the more tender.
- **Don't crowd the pan** when searing — work in batches so the chicken browns instead of steaming.

---

### Tip 6: Dal Gets Creamier the Right Way

From [Jake's Cooking School](https://jakescookingschool.substack.com/p/how-to-cook-dal-properly) and [Tea for Turmeric](https://www.teaforturmeric.com/masoor-dal/):

**For this week's Dal Makhani:**
- **Mash some lentils against the side of the pot** while simmering — this releases starch and creates creaminess. This is in the recipe, but do it thoroughly.
- **If mashing isn't enough** (lentils are stubborn), take 1 cup of the cooked dal with some liquid, blend until smooth, then return to the pot. This gives you creamy texture while keeping some whole lentils for body.
- **Skim the foam** that rises to the top during cooking — this keeps the dal cleaner-tasting.
- **The dal is ready when it has a consistency like porridge** — some lentils intact, some broken down to thicken. Don't serve it as thick as mashed potatoes; a little extra water at the end is better than too-dry dal.

---

### Tip 7: Don't Substitute Spices Casually

"Assuming all spice blends are the same or substituting one for another without understanding their distinct flavors" is a common mistake. "Garam masala is not the same as curry powder, for example."

— Shilpa Joshi, in [Mashed](https://www.mashed.com/1495300/mistakes-everyone-makes-cooking-indian-food/) (Jan 2024)

**This week's takeaway:** If you're missing garam masala, make the DIY blend from Part 1 of this guide. Do NOT use curry powder as a substitute — "curry powder is not an Indian spice blend" and was invented by the British. Garam masala goes in at the END of cooking (it's a finishing spice); curry powder would go in earlier. Different timing, different flavour.

---

### Tip 8: Know When to Add Garam Masala

"Garam masala is generally added towards the end of cooking to preserve its aromatic qualities."

— Monica Bhide, in [NPR Kitchen Window](https://www.npr.org/2011/12/07/143251451/the-crackling-spices-of-indian-tempering) (Dec 2011)

**This week's application:** In the Butter Chicken, garam masala goes into the marinade (raw) AND the simmering sauce. In the Rogan Josh and Dal Makhani, extra garam masala is stirred in at the very end. Do not add garam masala at the start of a long simmer — it loses its fragrance.

---

### Cooking Tip Summary — Quick Checklist

- [ ] **Measure all spices into bowls** before turning on the stove
- [ ] **Test oil heat** with a single cumin seed — it should sizzle immediately
- [ ] **Bloom spices** in hot oil 1-2 minutes until fragrant — never skip
- [ ] **Don't rush** — the Rogan Josh and Dal Makhani need their full simmer times
- [ ] **Garam masala at the end** — it's a finishing spice
- [ ] **Mash the dal** thoroughly against the pot while simmering
- [ ] **Pat chicken dry** before the yogurt marinade
- [ ] **If oil smokes or spices turn black** — start the tadka over
- [ ] **Turmeric stains** — wear an apron, clean spills immediately
