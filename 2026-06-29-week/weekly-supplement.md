## 1. Achiote / Recado Rojo

<img src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/51eqsJRvKqL.jpg" align="right" width="100">

**What is it?** It is a spice blend that comes from the Yucatán peninsula. The main ingredient is annatto seed (also called bijol), which gives a beautiful red-orange color to the meat. It is used to marinate cochinita pibil. It is like a dry rub or paste, with a Yucatán flavor.

**What does it look like?** It comes in small packages (usually 3.5 oz / 100g). It is a powder or paste of dark red to bright orange color. Sometimes it comes as a compact paste, sometimes as loose powder.

**What does it taste like?** It has an earthy flavor, slightly spicy but not strong. It combines notes of cumin, oregano, garlic, and pepper. The flavor is very distinctive — do not confuse achiote with chili powder. It is more like a savory dry rub.

**Where to buy:**
- 🟢 **Chedraui (Av. Juárez)** — Spice section, look for "recado rojo" or "achiote"
- 🟢 **Walmart (30 Av. y Calle 8)** — Mexican spices section
- 🟢 **Mega Soriana (30 Av. y Constituyentes)** — Spice section
- 🟢 **Any local market** — Ask for "achiote" or "recado rojo"

**Recommended brands:** El Yucateco, Mexicali, El Mexicano. **Check the label — some brands cut with wheat flour.**

**Substitutes (in order of preference):**
1. Make your own recado rojo at home (see instructions below) — 100% GF guaranteed
2. El Yucateco achiote paste (generally GF, but verify the label)
3. If you can't find anything: 2 tbsp sweet paprika + 1 tsp cumin + 1 tsp oregano + 1 tsp garlic powder + 1/2 tsp black pepper. It doesn't give the same color but the flavor is similar.

**How to make recado rojo at home (if the packaged one is not GF):**

If you are not sure that the packaged achiote is GF, you can make it yourself. You need annatto seeds (sold at DAC Market or at markets):

- 3 tbsp annatto seeds (bijol)
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- 1 tsp ground coriander (coriander seed)
- 1 tsp dried oregano
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1/2 tsp ground black pepper
- 1/2 tsp ground allspice
- 1/2 tsp ground cloves
- 1 tsp salt
- 2 tbsp sour orange juice (or orange juice + 1 tsp vinegar)
- 1 tsp white vinegar

**Instructions:** Toast the annatto seeds in a dry comal over medium heat for 2-3 minutes. Grind everything in a molcajete or blender until it becomes powder. Mix with the juice and vinegar until it forms a paste. Done.

**Difficulty:** 🟢 easy (any supermarket)

**⚠️ GF watch point:** Some commercial brands of recado rojo use wheat flour as a filler. **ALWAYS check the label.** Look for "gluten-free" or verify that it does not say "harina de trigo" or "wheat flour" in the ingredients. If you are not sure, make it at home with the recipe above.

## 7. Banana Leaves (optional, for the cochinita)

<img src="https://i0.wp.com/parce.ca/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/hojas-platano-_processed.png" align="right" width="100">

**What are they?** They are the large leaves of the banana plant. They are used in Yucatecan cooking to wrap the meat while it cooks — they give a slightly sweet and herbal flavor, and help keep the meat juicy. In traditional cochinita pibil, the meat is wrapped in banana leaves and cooked in an underground oven (pib). For our slow cooker version, they are optional but recommended.

**What do they look like?** They come frozen in flat packages. They are large leaves, dark green, almost black from the cold. They thaw at room temperature and become flexible and bright green.

**What do they taste like?** They are not eaten. They only give flavor and aroma to the meat — a slightly sweet, green, and herbal touch, like fresh grass but subtle.

**Where to buy:**
- 🟢 **Chedraui (Av. Juárez)** — Frozen section, look for "hojas de plátano"
- 🟢 **Walmart (30 Av. y Calle 8)** — Frozen or Mexican food section
- 🟢 **Local markets** — Sometimes sold fresh

**Substitutes:**
- **Do not use them.** The recipe works without banana leaves. Just put the meat directly in the slow cooker with the marinade. The flavor will be slightly different but just as good.

**Usage tip:** If you buy them frozen, thaw them at room temperature 1-2 hours before using. Before using them, pass them quickly over the stove flame (directly over the flame, 2 seconds per side) — this makes them more flexible and releases the aroma.

**Difficulty:** 🟢 easy (any supermarket with a frozen section)

**⚠️ GF watch point:** Banana leaves are naturally GF. No risk.

## 11. Habanero Hot Sauce (Yucateco or Frontera)

<img src="https://www.peppersofkeywest.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/07/el-yuc-red.jpg" align="right" width="100">

**What is it?** It is a hot sauce made with habanero chile. The habanero is one of the spiciest chiles in Mexico, but in sauce it is mixed with other ingredients so it is spicy but flavorful. It is served with cochinita pibil so each person can add the heat they want.

**What does it look like?** It comes in plastic bottles. The **El Yucateco** brand comes in different colored bottles depending on the type: red (red habanero), green (green habanero), yellow (Caribbean habanero). The **Frontera** brand comes in more elegant bottles. For cochinita, red or green work the same.

**What does it taste like?** Spicy, fruity, with a citrus touch. The habanero has a fruity flavor (like mango or tropical fruits) in addition to the heat. It is much spicier than jalapeño — use it with care.

**Where to buy:**
- 🟢 **Chedraui (Av. Juárez)** — Sauce and dressing section
- 🟢 **Walmart (30 Av. y Calle 8)** — Sauce section
- 🟢 **Mega Soriana (30 Av. y Constituyentes)** — Sauce section
- 🟢 **Any corner store** — It is very common

**Recommended brands:** El Yucateco (classic), Frontera (by Rick Bayless, more artisanal). Both are GF.

**Substitutes:**
1. Any habanero hot sauce
2. Chile de árbol sauce (less spicy)
3. If you do not want spice: omit it. The cochinita has plenty of flavor on its own.

**Difficulty:** 🟢 easy (any supermarket)

**⚠️ GF watch point:** El Yucateco and Frontera sauces are GF. Check the label of other brands. Avoid sauces that contain soy sauce — if any recipe calls for soy sauce, use tamari (which is GF).

## 13. Arroz Rojo (Mexican Red Rice) Ingredients

Arroz rojo is a classic Mexican side dish — rice fried in oil, then cooked with a blended tomato, onion, and garlic sauce in chicken broth. It is served alongside almost every meal in Mexico, including this week's dishes. The ingredients are simple but the technique is what makes it special.

**The ingredients you need:**

- **White rice (1.5 cups):** Medium-grain rice is more traditional for Mexican rice because the grains absorb flavor well and stay fluffy. But long-grain white rice works fine — that is what most people in Mexico use at home. **Do not use brown rice** — it takes much longer to cook and the texture is wrong.
- **Canned diced tomatoes (1 can, 15 oz):** Fire-roasted diced tomatoes are preferred because they add a smoky flavor. Regular diced tomatoes work too. This is the base of the red color and flavor.
- **White onion (1/2 small):** Blended with the tomatoes and garlic.
- **Garlic (2 cloves):** Blended with the tomatoes and onion.
- **Chicken broth (1.75 cups):** Used to cook the rice. Low-sodium is preferred so you can control the salt.
- **Vegetable oil (1.5 tbsp):** For frying the rice. Vegetable or canola oil works well. Olive oil is too heavy for this dish.
- **Serrano chiles (3):** They are slit lengthwise (not chopped) and cooked whole in the rice. They release a mild, spicy flavor into the rice and are **removed before serving**. They are not meant to be eaten.
- **Fresh parsley (1/4 cup):** This is **perejil** — flat-leaf parsley, the kind used in Italian cooking. **It is NOT cilantro.** They look similar (both are green leafy herbs) but the flavor is very different. Parsley is mild and slightly peppery. Cilantro is much more pungent and has a distinctive soapy flavor for some people. For arroz rojo, use parsley. If you only have cilantro, you can use it instead, but the flavor will be different.
- **Salt:** To taste.

**Where to buy:**
- 🟢 **Canned diced tomatoes:** Any Chedraui, Walmart, Mega Soriana — canned goods aisle
- 🟢 **White rice:** Any supermarket — rice aisle
- 🟢 **Serrano chiles:** Any Chedraui, Walmart, Mega Soriana — produce section
- 🟢 **Fresh parsley (perejil):** Any supermarket — produce section, refrigerated herb section. Look for "perejil liso" (flat-leaf parsley), not "perejil chino" (which is cilantro).
- 🟢 **Chicken broth:** Any supermarket — soup/broth aisle, or use bouillon powder with water

**Substitutes:**
- **Fire-roasted tomatoes:** Use regular diced tomatoes + 1/2 tsp smoked paprika for a smoky note
- **Fresh parsley:** Use cilantro (different flavor, but acceptable). Or use 1 tbsp dried parsley.
- **Serrano chiles:** Use jalapeño chiles (less spicy, more common)
- **Medium-grain rice:** Long-grain white rice is fine

**⚠️ Key technique — DO NOT SKIP THIS:**

The rice must be **fried in oil until it turns from translucent white to a milky, opaque white** before adding the tomato puree. This step is what keeps the grains separate and fluffy. Here is how:

1. Heat the oil in a wide pot (about 8 inches / 20 cm diameter — a wide pot, not a tall pot) over medium heat.
2. Add the uncooked rice. Stir constantly with a wooden spoon for about 5-7 minutes.
3. The rice will first turn translucent, then gradually turn white and milky. When most of the grains are milky white (not just translucent), it is ready.
4. **Now** add the blended tomato/onion/garlic puree. Stir for 2 minutes to cook the tomato.
5. Add the chicken broth, salt, and the slit serrano chiles. Bring to a boil.
6. Reduce heat to low, cover, and **cook for 20 minutes WITHOUT STIRRING.** Do not lift the lid, do not stir — stirring releases starch from the grains and makes the rice gummy and sticky.
7. After 20 minutes, turn off the heat. Let it rest covered for 5-10 minutes.
8. Remove the serrano chiles. Fluff the rice with a **fork** (never a spoon — a spoon smashes the grains). Sprinkle the chopped parsley on top.

**Difficulty:** 🟢 easy (any supermarket for ingredients; technique is medium-difficulty)

**⚠️ GF watch point:** All arroz rojo ingredients are naturally GF. Just check that your chicken broth is GF (some bouillon powders have gluten — see the GF watch section).

## 1. How to Rehydrate Dried Chiles for Chile Colorado

The chile colorado sauce uses dried guajillo, ancho, and árbol chiles. These are not used straight from the package — they must be rehydrated (softened in hot water) before blending into a sauce.

**According to Isabel Orozco-Moore at Isabel Eats** (https://www.isabeleats.com/chile-colorado-recipe/), the correct method is:

1. **Remove the stems and seeds.** Open each chile with scissors or your hands, take out the seeds, and discard the stem. Rinse the chiles under water to remove dust.
2. **Place the chiles and onion in a pot** and cover with water until completely submerged. Bring to a boil over high heat.
3. **Remove from heat immediately once boiling.** This is critical — do not let the chiles continue to boil. Cover the pot and let sit for 20 minutes to soften. Boiling chiles makes them bitter.
4. **Transfer the softened chiles and onion to a blender.** Add 1 cup of the chile-soaking water, oregano, salt, garlic, and cumin. Blend until smooth.

**Why not boil the chiles?** According to Isabel Eats, continuous boiling destroys the flavor compounds in dried chiles and makes them taste bitter. The gentle heat of just-br boiled water, then removed from the stove, is enough to soften them without damaging the flavor.

**Spice control:** The árbol chile is the hottest of the three. Keep its seeds in for medium-hot, or remove them for mild. For more heat, add 2-3 árbol chiles instead of 1.

## 2. How to Make Tinga — The Chipotle-Tomato Base

Tinga de pollo is shredded chicken in a smoky chipotle-tomato sauce. The key is building the sauce properly before adding the chicken.

**According to Mely Martínez at Mexico In My Kitchen** (https://www.mexicoinmykitchen.com/chicken-tinga-recipe/), the technique is:

1. **Cook the onions first.** Heat oil in a pan, add sliced onions, and stir-fry 3 minutes. Add garlic and cook 2 more minutes until fragrant. This builds the flavor base.
2. **Let the tomatoes release their juices.** Add cubed tomatoes and parsley. Lower the heat and cook 6-7 minutes until the tomatoes break down and form a sauce. If the tomatoes are not juicy enough, add a couple tablespoons of the chicken cooking liquid.
3. **Add chicken and chipotle last.** Add the shredded chicken and chopped chipotle peppers. Simmer 8 minutes until all flavors blend and the sauce thickens slightly.

**Chipotle heat control:** Mely notes that 2 chipotle peppers give a medium spice level. Use 1 for mild, 3 for hot. The chipotles in adobo are smoky and spicy — a little goes a long way. Chop them finely so they distribute evenly through the sauce.

**Tip from Mely:** "If you like your chicken tinga a little dry, let it cook a little longer." The longer you cook it, the more the sauce reduces and clings to the chicken.

## 3. How to Slow-Cook Pork Shoulder (for Cochinita Pibil)

Cochinita pibil uses pork shoulder (leg/picnic), which is a tough cut with a lot of connective tissue. If you cook it fast, it comes out tough and chewy. If you cook it low and slow, the connective tissue breaks down and the meat becomes soft as butter.

**According to J. Kenji López-Alt at Serious Eats** (https://www.seriouseats.com/ultra-crispy-slow-roasted-pork-shoulder-recipe), the science behind this is:

> "Beginning around 160°F (71°C), tough collagen begins to break down into tender, juicy gelatin. The hotter the meat, the faster this breakdown occurs."

But there is a trick: if you cook at very high temperature, the muscle fibers contract too much and squeeze out the juices, leaving the meat dry. The low, steady temperature of the slow cooker allows the collagen to break down slowly without the meat losing its juices.

**According to The Dizzy Cook** (https://thedizzycook.com/like-pulled-pork-cannot-lie/), the internal temperature of the pork should reach 195-205°F (91-96°C) for it to be tender and fall apart. Pork is safe to eat at 145°F (63°C), but for it to fall apart like cochinita, it needs to go higher.

**For our slow cooker recipe:**
- Cook on HIGH for 6 hours, as Rick Bayless's recipe says.
- Do not open the lid during cooking — every time you open it, you lose 20-30 minutes of cooking because the heat escapes.
- You will know it is ready when the meat falls apart easily with a fork. If it is still firm, let it go another hour.
- After cooking, remove the meat and skim excess fat from the juice. If there is a lot of juice (more than 2 cups), boil it in a pot until it reduces to 1 cup. This concentrates the flavor.
- Shred the meat with two forks. Mix with the reduced sauce.

## 4. How to Cook Calabacitas Without Making Them Mushy

Calabacitas con elote is a quick vegetable dish — zucchini, corn, tomato, onion, and serrano. The challenge is cooking the zucchini until tender without turning it to mush.

**According to Mely Martínez at Mexico In My Kitchen** (https://www.mexicoinmykitchen.com/corn-and-zucchini-mexican-style/), the technique is:

1. **Sauté the onion and garlic first** in oil for 1 minute — this builds the flavor base.
2. **Add the zucchini and cover the pan.** Covering creates steam, which cooks the zucchini evenly without browning. Cook 5 minutes.
3. **Add corn and serrano after 5 minutes.** Corn cooks faster than zucchini, so it goes in later. Stir to prevent browning. Cook 3 minutes.
4. **Add tomatoes last.** Tomatoes release water and can make the dish soggy if added too early. Add them, season with salt and pepper, cover, and simmer just until everything is tender — about 5 more minutes.

**Key rules:**
- **Do not overcook.** The total cooking time is about 13-15 minutes. If you cook longer, the zucchini gets soft and watery.
- **Cut zucchini into even cubes** (about 1/2 inch / 1 cm) so they cook at the same rate.
- **Do not add water** unless the tomatoes are very dry. The vegetables release their own liquid.

**Optional cheese:** Sprinkle crumbled queso fresco on top after cooking. The heat of the vegetables will soften it slightly. This is optional — the dish is delicious without it.

## 5. How to Cook Dried Beans Properly (for Frijoles Charros)

Frijoles charros uses dried pinto beans. You can use canned beans, but dried beans cooked at home taste much better, are cheaper, and you control the texture.

**According to Epicurious** (https://www.epicurious.com/expert-advice/soaking-salting-dried-bean-myths-article), there are many myths about beans. Here are the facts:

**Do you have to soak beans?** It is not mandatory, but it helps. Soaked beans cook faster and more evenly, and are easier to digest (less gas). If you do not soak them, you only need 10 more minutes of cooking.

**Quick soak method (if you forgot to soak them the night before):**
1. Put the beans in a large pot. Cover with water at least 5 cm above the beans.
2. Bring to a boil over high heat. When it starts boiling, count 2 minutes.
3. Turn off the heat, cover the pot, and let rest 1 hour.
4. Drain the water and rinse the beans.

**Long soak method (the night before):**
1. Put the beans in a large bowl. Cover with water at least 7-8 cm above (beans absorb a lot of water).
2. Let soak overnight (8 hours) at room temperature.
3. Drain and rinse before cooking.

**According to Isabel Eats** (https://www.isabeleats.com/how-to-cook-pinto-beans-on-the-stove/), you should check the beans before cooking:

> "Sort through the beans and discard any that are cracked or split, as well as any rocks or debris."

**Stovetop cooking:**
1. Put the soaked beans in a pot with 3 cups of water, 1/4 onion, and 2 garlic cloves.
2. Cook over medium-high heat until boiling, then reduce to medium heat.
3. Cook for 1.5-2 hours until tender. Test one — if it is soft inside, they are ready.
4. Do not add salt at the beginning — according to Isabel Eats, "adding salt too early increases cooking time and toughens the beans." Add salt at the end.

**Slow cooker cooking (recommended for this week):**
1. Put the soaked beans in the slow cooker with 3 cups of water, 1/4 onion, and 2 garlic cloves.
2. Cook on HIGH for 5-6 hours or on LOW for 7-8 hours.
3. Add salt at the end.

**Tip:** If you use canned beans (2 cans of 425g), drain and rinse them. Use 1 cup extra water instead of the cooking broth. The flavor will not be as deep, but it works if you do not have time.

## 6. How to Char Tomatoes, Onion, and Garlic on a Comal

Charring (tatemar in Spanish) means roasting ingredients directly on a hot comal or griddle until they are burnt on the outside. This is a fundamental technique in Mexican cooking that adds smoky depth to salsas and sauces.

**According to Pati Jinich** (https://patijinich.com/charring_ingredients/), Mexican chef and host of the PBS show *Pati's Mexican Table*:

> "Charring tomatoes, aside from concentrating and deepening their flavor, brings out their sweetness and juices. To char tomatoes, place them directly on an already hot comal or griddle over medium heat, for about 10 minutes, turning them a couple of times."

**To char tomatoes:**
1. Heat a comal or cast iron skillet over medium-high heat. Do not add oil — it chars dry.
2. Place the whole tomatoes (with the stem end) directly on the comal.
3. Turn them every 3-4 minutes. They will flatten, release juice, and turn black on the outside. That is what we want.
4. They are ready when they are completely soft inside, the skin is blistered and black, and they have released their juices (about 10 minutes).

**To char onion:**
1. Cut the onion into quarters (large wedges) with the skin on.
2. Place the wedges directly on the hot comal.
3. Turn them until they are black on all sides (about 15 minutes).

**To char garlic:**
1. **Do not peel the garlic.** Leave it in its skin.
2. **Prick each clove with a toothpick** (make a hole with a toothpick) so it does not pop on the comal.
3. Place the unpeeled cloves on the comal.
4. Turn them until the skin is black and the garlic inside is soft (about 10-15 minutes).
5. When cool, peel them — the skin comes off easily.

**According to Frida's Cocina** (https://www.fridascocinatx.com/blog/traditional-mexican-cooking-techniques-mastering-nixtamalization-molcajete-grinding-and-comal-cooking), the comal should be preheated before use:

> "Preheat the comal over medium heat for several minutes, allowing it to reach an even temperature. Test the temperature by sprinkling a few drops of water — they should sizzle and evaporate quickly."

**Why char instead of roasting in the oven or boiling?** The direct heat of the comal burns the natural sugar in the tomatoes and onion, creating a smoky, caramelized flavor that cannot be obtained any other way. It is the difference between a flavorful salsa and a flat one.

## 7. Mise en Place for Mexican Cooking (Organize Before You Start)

Mise en place (pronounced "meez ahn plahs") is a French term that means "everything in its place." It is the practice of preparing and organizing **all** ingredients before you start cooking. In Mexican cooking, where many recipes have multiple steps, this is essential.

**According to Made In Cookware** (https://madeincookware.com/blogs/mise-en-place):

> "Mise en place is a chef's practice meaning 'everything in its place.' Read the recipe, then prepare and organize all ingredients and tools before you start cooking."

**According to All-Clad** (https://www.all-clad.com/blog/post/Mise-en-Place-Master-Your-Prep-Flow):

> "Ingredients are grouped logically — by step or by dish — and tools are placed within easy reach. This keeps your workspace clear and your focus where it belongs."

**How to apply mise en place this week:**

Before you start cooking any dish, do this:

1. **Read the recipe from beginning to end.** Understand all the steps before starting.
2. **Weigh and measure all ingredients.** Put each ingredient in a small bowl or plate.
3. **Chop everything you need to chop** before turning on the stove: onion, garlic, tomato, cilantro, chiles.
4. **Group ingredients by step.** For example, for the chile colorado:
   - Group 1 (chiles): guajillo (8), ancho (2), árbol (1)
   - Group 2 (sauce aromatics): onion, garlic, oregano, cumin, salt
   - Group 3 (meat): beef chuck, cornstarch, salt, pepper
   - Group 4 (liquid): beef broth, bay leaf
5. **Prepare your tools:** clean blender, pots and pans, wooden spoon, spatula.
6. **Line everything up** left to right, in the order you will use it.

**For the chile colorado especially:** The chile sauce has multiple steps (soak, blend, strain) and the meat needs to be browned in batches. If you prepare everything before turning on the stove, the process flows smoothly.

**Practical tip:** Use small bowls or measuring cups. Put all the bowls on a tray or sheet pan so you can move them all together from the counter to the stove. This way you do not forget anything.

## 8. How to Make Fluffy Arroz Rojo (Mexican Red Rice)

Arroz rojo looks simple — it is just rice with tomato — but the difference between fluffy, restaurant-style Mexican rice and gummy, sticky rice comes down to technique. The four most important rules, according to chef Rick Bayless (https://www.rickbayless.com/recipe/red-tomato-rice/), are:

**Rule 1: Fry the rice in oil before adding any liquid.**

According to Rick Bayless, frying the uncooked rice in oil is the step that makes Mexican rice different from steamed rice. The hot oil coats each grain and partially gelatinizes the starch on the outside, which:
- Keeps the grains separate and fluffy after cooking
- Prevents the rice from sticking together in a mushy clump
- Gives the rice a slightly nutty, toasted flavor

You fry the rice until it changes color from translucent/pearly white to a milky, opaque white (about 5-7 minutes of constant stirring over medium heat). Do not let it brown — you want white, not golden.

**Rule 2: Do NOT stir the rice while it simmers.**

This is the rule most home cooks break. According to Bayless, once you add the broth and bring the rice to a simmer, you cover the pot and **leave it alone**. Do not stir, do not peek, do not lift the lid for the full 20 minutes of cooking.

Why? Every time you stir rice while it cooks, you release starch from the grains into the liquid. That starch makes the rice gummy and sticky, like risotto or arroz con leche. Mexican rice is the opposite — it should be light, fluffy, with each grain separate.

If you lift the lid, you also lose steam, which lowers the cooking temperature and makes the rice cook unevenly. Trust the process: 20 minutes covered, no peeking.

**Rule 3: Use a wide pot (about 8 inches / 20 cm diameter), not a tall pot.**

A wide pot means the rice spreads out in a thin, even layer on the bottom. This:
- Cooks the rice evenly (no raw grains in the center)
- Allows steam to escape properly so the rice does not get soggy
- Gives the rice the characteristic dry, fluffy texture of Mexican restaurant rice

A tall, narrow pot traps steam and makes the rice wetter and stickier. If you only have a tall pot, you can still make it work — just use less rice and be extra careful not to overcook it.

**Rule 4: Fluff with a fork after cooking, never with a spoon.**

When the rice is done and has rested for 5-10 minutes off the heat (still covered), remove the lid and use a **fork** to fluff the grains. A fork separates the grains gently without crushing them. A spoon smashes and breaks the grains, releasing starch and making the rice sticky.

After fluffing, remove the whole serrano chiles with a fork or tongs — they were for flavor only and are not eaten. Sprinkle the chopped fresh parsley (perejil) over the top.

**Practical tip:** If your arroz rojo turns out gummy the first time, do not worry. It is one of those techniques that takes a few tries to master. The most common mistake is stirring during simmering — once you stop doing that, the rice will be much fluffier.
