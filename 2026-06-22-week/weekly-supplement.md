## Ghee (Clarified Butter)

<img src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/S/aplus-media-library-service-media/54b77221-6020-49e7-8aa3-cf57b3d02299.__AC_SR166,182___.jpg" align="right" width="100" alt="Ghee">

**What is it?** It is butter that has been cooked over low heat until all the water evaporates and the milk solids (the white parts that burn) separate and are removed. What remains is a golden, pure oil with a nutty flavor. It is like lard but made from butter — and it withstands much higher temperatures without burning.

**What does it taste like?** Like toasted butter, with a nutty undertone. It is richer and deeper than regular butter. It gives a characteristic flavor to Indian food that cannot be imitated with regular oil.

**Difficulty:** 🟡 medium — Found in the international section of Walmart or Chedraui, but you can also make it at home (see below).

**Where to buy:**
- Walmart (30 Av. y Calle 8) — international section, Gopala brand or similar
- Chedraui Selecto (Plaza Las Américas) — international foods section
- DAC Market — sometimes they have Indian brands
- Amazon Mexico — search for "ghee" or "mantequilla clarificada"

**Substitutes (in order of preference):**
1. **Make ghee at home** (see instructions below) — easy and cheaper
2. Regular butter (doesn't withstand as much heat, but works)
3. Vegetable oil + 1 teaspoon of butter for flavor (only if there's no other option)

**How to make ghee at home:**
Put 250g of unsalted butter in a pot over medium-low heat. Don't cover. The butter will melt, then bubble. After about 10–15 minutes you will see particles accumulating at the bottom — these are the milk solids. When those particles turn golden brown and the butter smells like toasted nuts, turn off the heat. Strain through a fine sieve or cheesecloth. The golden liquid that passes through is the ghee. Discard the solids. Ghee lasts weeks at room temperature or months in the refrigerator.

## Kasuri Methi (Dried Fenugreek Leaves)

<img src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/81a7rzd1dhL.jpg" align="right" width="100" alt="Kasuri Methi">

**What is it?** These are leaves from a plant called fenugreek (in Mexico, the seeds of this plant are known as "fenugreek seeds" or sometimes found in health food stores). The leaves are dried and used as a seasoning. It is a CRITICAL ingredient this week — it appears in 4 of the 5 dishes. Do not skip it.

**What does it taste like?** It has a bitter and aromatic flavor that is hard to describe — like a mix between dried celery, toasted cilantro leaves, and a bitter touch like epazote. Only a small amount is used (1–2 tablespoons per dish). When you add it to the curry at the end of cooking, it transforms the flavor completely — it gives that "Indian restaurant" flavor that cannot be achieved without it.

**How is it used?** Take a handful of leaves between your palms and rub them to crumble into small pieces. Add them in the last minutes of cooking.

**Difficulty:** 🔴 difficult — Not found in normal supermarkets. You have to go to DAC Market or order it on Amazon.

**Where to buy:**
- **DAC Market** (30 Av. entre Calle 14 y 14 Bis) — ask for "kasuri methi" or "dried fenugreek leaves." This is your best local option.
- **Amazon Mexico** — search for "kasuri methi." They sell brands like Everest, MDH, or Spicy World. A 100g packet costs around $100–150 MXN and lasts for many weeks.
- If you go to Cancún, there are Indian stores like "India Mart" that surely carry it.

**Substitutes (in order of preference):**
1. There is no perfect substitute. Kasuri methi has a unique flavor.
2. If you absolutely cannot find it: use 1 teaspoon of fenugreek seeds (methi seeds) dry-toasted and ground. It's not the same but gives some of the flavor.
3. As a last resort: omit it. The curry will still be good, but it will lack that authentic restaurant flavor.

**Tip:** Buy 2 packets if you find it. It keeps well for months in a sealed jar.

## Kashmiri Red Chili Powder

<img src="https://images-na.ssl-images-amazon.com/images/I/810XQGM2zpL.jpg" align="right" width="100" alt="Kashmiri Chili Powder">

**What is it?** It is a chili powder from the Kashmir region in northern India. It is famous for giving an intense, beautiful red color to food without making it spicy. It is like having a chili that paints red but barely has any heat — think of something between paprika and ancho chili powder, but redder.

**What does it taste like?** Slightly smoky, earthy, with very mild heat (1,000–2,000 Scoville, comparable to dried poblano). Regular Mexican chili powder is much spicier.

**Difficulty:** 🟡 medium — Can be found at DAC Market or Amazon. Not in normal supermarkets.

**Where to buy:**
- **DAC Market** — ask for "Kashmiri chili powder" or "chile en polvo de Cachemira"
- **Amazon Mexico** — search for "Kashmiri red chili powder." Brands: Laxmi, Rani, Spicewalla.
- Chedraui Selecto sometimes carries it in their international spices section.

**Homemade substitute (very easy):**
Mix **2 parts paprika** with **1 part cayenne pepper (chile en polvo)**. For example: 2 teaspoons of paprika + 1 teaspoon of cayenne = 3 teaspoons of Kashmiri chili substitute. The paprika gives the red color and the cayenne gives the heat. Adjust the cayenne if you want it less spicy.

This week you need approximately 4 teaspoons total. If you can't find real Kashmiri chili, use the substitute — it works very well.

## Garam Masala

<img src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/51hnY9AlthL.jpg" align="right" width="100" alt="Garam Masala">

**What is it?** It is the most important spice blend in Indian cooking. "Garam" means "hot" and "masala" means "blend" — but not spicy hot, rather warming to the body according to Ayurvedic medicine. It is like the Mexican "recado" or "adobo" but Indian: a blend of toasted and ground spices used at the end of cooking to add aroma.

**What does it taste like?** Warm, aromatic, complex — with notes of cinnamon, clove, cardamom, and cumin. It is not spicy. Used in small amounts (1–2 teaspoons per dish).

**Difficulty:** 🟢 easy — Found in the international section of most large supermarkets.

**Where to buy:**
- **Walmart** (30 Av. y Calle 8) — international foods section or spice aisle
- **Chedraui** (Av. Juárez) — international section
- **Chedraui Selecto** (Plaza Las Américas) — better selection
- **DAC Market** — they have authentic Indian brands (Everest, MDH, Catch)
- **Amazon Mexico** — many options

**Substitute: make it yourself (homemade recipe):**
If you can't find garam masala or want a fresher one, make it. Dry-toast (no oil) in a comal or skillet over medium heat:
- 2 tablespoons cumin seeds
- 2 tablespoons coriander seeds
- 1 tablespoon black peppercorns
- 1 tablespoon whole cloves
- 1 tablespoon green cardamom seeds (open the pods and use the seeds)
- 2 cinnamon sticks (broken into pieces)
- 1 bay leaf

Toast 2–3 minutes, stirring constantly until fragrant. Let cool. Grind everything in a mortar, blender, or coffee grinder until it becomes a fine powder. Store in a sealed jar. Lasts 2–3 months.

## Asafoetida (Hing) — ⚠️ GLUTEN CAUTION

<img src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/31UOpAy1k6L.jpg" align="right" width="100" alt="Asafoetida / Hing">

**What is it?** It is a dried, ground resin that comes from a plant. It has a very strong and unpleasant smell raw — some say it smells like rotten garlic. But when fried in hot oil, it transforms completely: the smell disappears and it gives a mild flavor that combines garlic and onion. It is used in tiny amounts — a pinch.

**Why is it important this week?** It is used in the rajma masala (red kidney bean curry). But there is a problem: **most commercial brands of hing contain wheat flour** as an anti-caking agent. This means it is NOT gluten-free.

**Difficulty:** 🔴 difficult — And additionally, you need a certified gluten-free version.

**Where to buy:**
- **Amazon Mexico** — search for "hing gluten free" or "asafoetida gluten free." The brand "Food to Live" sells pure gluten-free hing.
- **DAC Market** — they might have it, but ask if it's pure or contains wheat.

**Substitute (recommended for this week):**
**Simply omit it.** The rajma masala is perfectly fine without hing. The recipe already says it is optional. Don't complicate yourself looking for gluten-free hing — the curry tastes just as good without it.

If you want the flavor that hing provides: add 1 extra clove of garlic, finely chopped, to the curry. It's not the same, but it gives a similar depth.

<img src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/41HPnApSpjL.jpg" align="right" width="100" alt="Basmati Rice">

## Greek Yogurt / Hung Curd

<img src="https://www.vegrecipesofindia.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/hung-curd-hung-yogurt.jpg" align="right" width="100" alt="Hung Curd / Greek Yogurt">

**What is it?** Greek yogurt is regular yogurt that has had the whey (the liquid) drained from it. It becomes thicker and creamier. In Indian cooking it is called "hung curd" and is used for marinades because its thickness makes the spices stick to the meat.

**Why not regular yogurt?** Regular yogurt has too much liquid and the marinade becomes runny. Greek yogurt sticks to the meat and coats it better.

**Difficulty:** 🟢 easy — Greek yogurt is found in all supermarkets.

**Where to buy:**
- **Walmart, Chedraui, Mega Soriana** — look for "yogur griego natural" (plain, no flavor, no sugar). Brands: Siggi's, Chobani, Fage, or store brands.
- You need 1 large tub (500g) — you will use ⅞ cup for the tandoori + ¾ cup for the lamb marinade.

**How to make hung curd at home (if you can only find regular yogurt):**
Put plain yogurt in a clean cloth or cheesecloth over a strainer. Place the strainer over a bowl. Let it drain in the refrigerator for 4–6 hours or overnight. The liquid that falls into the bowl is the whey — discard it. What remains in the cloth is the hung curd, thick like cream cheese.

## ⚠️ Gluten Watch Points This Week

All the Indian food this week is **naturally gluten-free** — none of the dishes use flour, bread, or wheat-based thickeners. The risk comes only from processed ingredients:

1. **Asafoetida (hing):** Most commercial brands contain wheat flour. **Solution: omit it.** The rajma masala is perfectly fine without it.

2. **Madras curry powder:** Some brands use wheat flour as a filler. **Solution:** check the label. TRS and East End brands are generally gluten-free. Or make it yourself (see recipe above).

3. **Kashmiri chili powder:** Should be pure ground chili, but some cheap brands add fillers. **Solution:** check that the only ingredient is "chili" or "red chili."

4. **Garam masala:** Should be pure spices. **Solution:** check the label or make it yourself.

5. **Ghee:** Pure clarified butter is gluten-free. But some cheap brands add things. **Solution:** buy pure ghee or make it at home with unsalted butter.

**Golden rule:** If a processed ingredient doesn't say "gluten-free" or "sin gluten" on the label, and has a long ingredient list with things you don't recognize, don't use it. Make it yourself or find another brand.

## 1. How to Marinate Lamb in Yogurt (Overnight Marinade Technique)

**Dishes where it is used:** Lamb Korma, Tandoori Chicken

The yogurt marinade is the foundation of Indian meat cooking. It is not like marinating in lemon or vinegar — yogurt works differently.

**Why does it work?** Yogurt contains **lactic acid**, which is a mild acid. Unlike lemon juice or vinegar (which are strong acids and can "cook" the meat on the outside while leaving it tough inside), lactic acid penetrates slowly and tenderizes the meat fibers without breaking them down. Additionally, the calcium in yogurt activates enzymes in the meat that break down collagen (the connective tissue that makes meat tough).

According to **Serious Eats** (one of the most respected cooking sites, known for its scientific testing of cooking techniques): "Lactic acid increases the amount of water absorbed by meat, and helps the meat hold onto it through the cooking process." This means the meat comes out juicier. In their tests, lamb marinated in yogurt overnight cooked in 40 minutes to the same tenderness as unmarinated lamb that took 1.5 hours.
— *Source: Serious Eats, "The Science of Yogurt Marinades," https://www.seriouseats.com/the-science-of-yogurt-marinades*

**Dr. Joseph Frank**, a food science professor at the University of Georgia, explains in **Epicurious**: "Lactic acid is a kind of mild acid... it's reacting with the food in a more gentle way. The pH is not going as low, and it's not doing as many changes to the protein's structure." This means it is nearly impossible to over-marinate meat in yogurt — you can leave it 24 or 48 hours without ruining it.
— *Source: Epicurious, "How Does a Yogurt Marinade Work?", https://www.epicurious.com/expert-advice/how-to-make-a-yogurt-marinade-for-meat-article*

**Practical tips for this week:**
- **Saturday night:** Prepare both marinades (lamb and chicken). Coat the meat thoroughly with the marinade — use your hands (with gloves) to massage the spices into every piece. Refrigerate overnight.
- **Use Greek yogurt** (or hung curd). Regular yogurt has too much liquid and the marinade becomes runny.
- **For the lamb:** Cut into 2-inch (5 cm) pieces. Smaller pieces dry out; larger pieces don't marinate inside.
- **For the chicken:** Make 2–3 deep slits in each thigh with a knife. This helps the marinade penetrate to the center of the meat.
- **Take the meat out of the refrigerator 45 minutes before cooking.** Cold meat cooks unevenly. Let it come to room temperature first.

## 2. How to Make Tandoori Chicken in the Oven (Without a Tandoor)

**Dish where it is used:** Tandoori Chicken

A tandoor is a cylindrical clay oven that reaches temperatures of 480°C (900°F) — much hotter than any home oven. The chicken cooks in minutes with a smoky, charred flavor that is hard to replicate. But you can get very close.

**The secret is extreme heat.** According to **Swasthi Shreekanth** of Indian Healthy Recipes (one of the most trusted Indian home cooks on the internet, with 20+ years of experience): "Any kind of heat source is good enough to grill/roast the chicken — an oven, stovetop, air fryer or direct fire." The key is using the highest temperature your oven can produce.
— *Source: Swasthi's Recipes, "Tandoori Chicken Recipe (Smoky Restaurant Style)," https://www.indianhealthyrecipes.com/tandoori-chicken-recipe/*

**RecipeTin Eats** (one of the most popular recipe sites in the world, with recipes tested by millions of readers) explains: "There's no denying that one of the reasons why we love Tandoori chicken is the signature smoky chargrilled flavour that can only be achieved in a clay tandoor built to withhold fierce charcoal heat. But you can get very close in a home oven with high temperature and letting charred spots form."
— *Source: RecipeTin Eats, "Oven Baked Tandoori Chicken," https://www.recipetineats.com/oven-baked-tandoori-chicken/*

**Step-by-step technique to replicate the tandoor in your oven:**

1. **Preheat the oven to maximum** — 240°C (470°F) for at least 15 minutes before. The oven must be truly hot when you put the chicken in.

2. **Use a wire rack over a baking tray.** Line a tray with aluminum foil. Place a metal wire rack over the tray and spray it with oil. The rack allows hot air to circulate around the entire chicken — this mimics how chicken hangs in a real tandoor. The juices drip onto the tray below.

3. **Don't leave excess marinade on the chicken.** When you take the chicken out of the marinade, let it drain well. If there is too much marinade, it will burn at that temperature and turn bitter.

4. **Bake 12–15 minutes per side.** Flip the chicken halfway through. Baste with chili oil (mix of Kashmiri chili powder + oil) or with leftover marinade.

5. **The finishing touch: the broiler.** When the chicken is cooked, move it to the top rack and broil for 3–4 minutes until you see charred spots. **Watch it closely** — the line between "perfectly charred" and "burnt" is very thin. These black spots are what give the tandoor flavor.

6. **The chicken is done when** the thermometer reads 75°C (165–170°F) in the thickest part.

**Smoking trick (optional):** If you want the smoky flavor of the tandoor, heat a piece of natural charcoal red-hot on the stove. Place it in a small metal bowl, set it in the center of the plate with the cooked chicken, pour 1 teaspoon of ghee or oil over the charcoal, and cover everything with a large lid for 2–3 minutes. The smoke will infuse the chicken. (Source: Swasthi's Recipes.)

**For reheating:** The air fryer at 180°C (350°F) for 5 minutes restores the charred texture. Much better than the microwave.

## 3. The Bhuna Technique (Frying Spices Intensively Before Adding Meat)

**Dish where it is used:** Beef Bhuna

"Bhuna" means "to fry" in Hindi/Bengali. It is not a dish — it is a **cooking technique**. The idea is to fry spices in hot oil until they release all their flavor and form a paste, then add the meat to cook in that concentrated paste. The result is a curry with a thick sauce that clings to the meat — not a watery broth.

**How is it different from a normal curry?** In a normal curry, you add water or stock to make a liquid sauce. In a bhuna, **you add very little liquid** — the meat cooks in its own juices and in the spice paste. The sauce concentrates by reduction, not by adding liquid.

According to **Tastes Delicious**: "Bhuna refers to a specific style of cooking, popular in both India and Bangladesh, where spices are fried in hot oil before meats, vegetables and other ingredients are added, but little or no liquid is added."
— *Source: Tastes Delicious, "The Art of Bhuna: India's Flavor-Packed Cooking Technique," https://tastesdelicious.com/bhuna/*

**Nicky's Kitchen Sanctuary** explains it this way: "The word 'Bhuna' refers to the cooking method, and means 'to fry'. Usually the spices are fried gently before adding the meat. Originating from Bengal, bhuna is a hot curry that doesn't come swimming in sauce. It's a thick sauce, with just enough to cling to the meat."
— *Source: Nicky's Kitchen Sanctuary, "Lamb Bhuna," https://www.kitchensanctuary.com/lamb-bhuna/*

**Swasthi's Recipes** adds: "The word 'Bhuna' refers to a specific cooking method which means 'To Fry' or 'Sauté'. Usually a basic onion tomato masala known as Bhuna Masala is first prepared by gently frying the spices, onions and tomatoes until aromatic."
— *Source: Swasthi's Recipes, "Chicken Bhuna Masala Recipe," https://www.indianhealthyrecipes.com/chicken-bhuna-masala/*

**Step-by-step technique for the beef bhuna:**

1. **Heat the oil well** before adding the whole spices (cardamom, bay leaves, cinnamon, fenugreek). Fry them 30–60 seconds until they release their aroma. If they burn, discard the oil and start over.

2. **Brown the onions over high heat** — 10–15 minutes. They must be golden brown, not translucent. This step is crucial: browned onions give the bhuna its color and deep flavor. Don't rush it.

3. **Add the beef and fry over high heat** until browned on the outside — about 10 minutes. Don't cook it fully here, just sear it so it retains its juices.

4. **Add the ground spices** (curry, garam masala, coriander, cumin, chili, turmeric) and mix well. Cook 1–2 minutes — raw spices taste bitter, they need to be cooked.

5. **Add the tomatoes** and cover. Cook on low-medium heat 5 minutes until the tomatoes break down.

6. **Now the key part: the long cook.** Cover and simmer on low heat 1–1.5 hours until the beef is tender. **If it looks dry, add water — but only a little at a time.** The bhuna should be thick, not watery.

7. **At the end,** if the sauce is too thick, add a little water to loosen it to your preference. Boil 7–10 more minutes for the flavors to integrate.

**Sign it's ready:** When the sauce separates from the oil (you can see the oil shining at the edges of the mixture), the bhuna is at its peak. This is called "masala ready" in Hindi.

## 4. How to Cook Dried Red Kidney Beans (And Why It's Dangerous If Undercooked)

**Dish where it is used:** Rajma Masala

⚠️ **IMPORTANT WARNING:** Raw or undercooked red kidney beans contain a toxin called **phytohaemagglutinin (PHA)** that causes nausea, vomiting, and severe diarrhea. This is not a myth — it is a real risk documented by food safety agencies worldwide.

**The Centre for Food Safety of Hong Kong** (a government food safety agency) explains: "Red kidney beans (Phaseolus vulgaris) contain the highest levels of phytohaemagglutinin. Consumption of raw beans can cause phytohaemagglutinin poisoning. Cooking with moist heat can remove the toxicity. Beans should be soaked and boiled thoroughly in fresh water (100°C)."
— *Source: Centre for Food Safety (Hong Kong), "Phytohaemagglutinin Poisoning," https://www.cfs.gov.hk/english/multimedia/multimedia_pub/multimedia_pub_fsf_208_01.html*

A medical case documented in **PubMed Central** (the medical database of the U.S. National Institutes of Health): An 8-year-old girl suffered hypovolemic shock and kidney damage after eating red kidney beans that "were soaked for only a few hours and boiled within a stew for approximately 15–20 minutes, which is insufficient to fully degrade PHA compared to guidelines recommending >12 h of soaking and vigorous boiling for at least 10 min in water."
— *Source: PMC/NIH, "Severe Red Kidney Bean Toxicity in an 8-Year-Old Girl," https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12922287/*

**StateFoodSafety** summarizes it this way: "The good news is that the toxin can be deactivated by simply boiling the raw beans for ten minutes. The FDA also recommends soaking the beans for five hours to remove any residual toxins and then tossing the water out."
— *Source: StateFoodSafety, "Toxic Beans," https://www.statefoodsafety.com/Resources/Resources/toxic-beans*

**ScienceDirect** (scientific database) adds a crucial warning: "The cooking procedure is very important and the use of 'slow cookers' or other procedures operating at a relatively low temperature may not destroy the glycoprotein lectin; and warming might increase the toxicity of these foods."
— *Source: ScienceDirect, "Phytohaemagglutinin," https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/phytohaemagglutinin*

**Golden rules for cooking red kidney beans this week:**

1. **Soak overnight (8–12 hours).** Saturday night: put 2 cups of dried red kidney beans in a large bowl. Cover with plenty of water (at least 5 cm above the beans, because they will absorb a lot). Leave them overnight.

2. **Discard the soaking water.** Do not use it for cooking — it contains part of the toxin.

3. **Boil vigorously for at least 10 minutes.** Drain the soaked beans, put them in a pot with 7–8 cups of fresh water. Bring to a rolling boil. **Maintain a vigorous boil for 10 full minutes.** This destroys the toxin. After that, you can lower the heat.

4. **Simmer 45–60 more minutes** until the beans are completely soft — they should fall apart when you bite them. If they have any firmness, cook longer. It is better to overcook than undercook.

5. **⚠️ If using a slow cooker:** FIRST boil the beans vigorously on the stove for 10 minutes. THEN transfer to the slow cooker. Never put raw or soaked red kidney beans directly in the slow cooker without boiling them first — the slow cooker temperature is not high enough to destroy the toxin.

6. **Safe alternative: canned beans.** If you don't want to take the risk, use 4 cans of red kidney beans (15 oz each). Canned beans are already fully cooked and safe. Drain and rinse them before using.

**How to tell if the beans are properly cooked?** Smash one between your fingers — it should fall apart without resistance. If it offers any resistance, cook 15 more minutes and test again.

## 5. How to Shallow-Fry Cauliflower for Aloo Gobi (Preventing Mushiness)

**Dish where it is used:** Aloo Gobi

The most common mistake with aloo gobi is that the cauliflower turns out soggy and mushy. The solution is to **fry the cauliflower and potatoes separately before adding them to the curry.** This gives them a firm texture and crispy edges that hold up to the sauce without falling apart.

**Priya Lakshminarayan** of Cookilicious (author of *The Essential Vegan Indian Cookbook* and the source of our recipe) explains: "If they become soggy or mushy, it will ruin the taste and texture of the curry. That is why frying them first helps. Frying the vegetables gives them a firmer texture, prevents them from becoming soggy, and also brings a whole new flavor dimension. Also, do not cover the pan when frying the veggies."
— *Source: Cookilicious, "Vegan and Gluten-Free Aloo Gobi Recipe," https://cookilicious.com/aloo-gobi-recipe/*

**Manali** of Cook With Manali (a very popular Indian home cook) adds: "One common problem with aloo gobi is that when you cook it, the cauliflower becomes soggy, and you don't want that. You want the veggies to be cooked and tender but not soggy. To retain the texture of the potatoes and cauliflower, I like to pan fry them first in a little oil until they have some brown spots. Don't steam the vegetables as that will make them soggy."
— *Source: Cook With Manali, "Aloo Gobi - Potatoes & Cauliflower," https://www.cookwithmanali.com/aloo-gobi/*

**Swasthi's Recipes** gives more detail: "A perfectly made aloo gobi has soft cooked potatoes & slightly crunchy cauliflower that's not soggy. In a lot of standard Indian restaurants, both these veggies are mostly deep fried until golden and crisp... Deep frying the veggies makes a huge difference as they cook down to a lighter texture and bring out the natural flavor."
— *Source: Swasthi's Recipes, "Aloo Gobi Recipe," https://www.indianhealthyrecipes.com/aloo-gobi-recipe/*

**Step-by-step technique:**

1. **Cut the cauliflower into medium florets** (not too small — small ones fall apart). Cut the potatoes into medium wedges of similar size. They should be similar in size so they cook evenly.

2. **Heat 4 tablespoons of ghee or oil** in a large pan over medium-high heat.

3. **Fry the potatoes first** — sprinkle with chili powder, kasuri methi, and cilantro. Fry 7–8 minutes until golden and almost cooked. Remove and set aside.

4. **Add 4 more tablespoons of ghee/oil** to the same pan. Fry the cauliflower with more chili, kasuri methi, and cilantro. Fry 8–10 minutes until the edges are golden and crispy. **DO NOT cover the pan.** Remove and set aside.

5. **Make the masala** (the curry base) in a large pot: oil, cumin seeds, ginger, garlic, green chiles, onion, tomato, spices.

6. **At the end,** add the fried potatoes and cauliflower to the masala. Fold gently to coat — don't stir roughly or they will fall apart.

**Air fryer option:** If you want to use less oil, put the cauliflower and potatoes in the air fryer with 2 tablespoons of oil and the spices. At 200°C (400°F) for 15 minutes, tossing halfway. They come out crispy with much less oil.

**Golden rules:**
- **Never cover the pan** when frying cauliflower — steam softens it.
- **Don't use frozen cauliflower** — it releases too much water and turns mushy.
- **Cut everything the same size** so it cooks evenly.
- **Fry separately** (potatoes first, then cauliflower) because cauliflower releases water and would lower the oil temperature.

## 6. How to Temper Whole Spices in Hot Oil (Tadka Technique)

**Dishes where it is used:** Lamb Korma, Beef Bhuna, Rajma Masala, Aloo Gobi, Rice

**Tadka** (also called chaunk, baghar, or phodni) is the most important technique in Indian cooking. It is the first step of almost every dish this week. It consists of heating oil or ghee and adding whole spices (cardamom, cinnamon, cloves, cumin, bay leaves) so they release their essential oils and aroma.

**NPR** (National Public Radio, one of the most trusted journalistic sources in the U.S.) explains it this way: "Tadka translates as 'tempering.' It is a method widely used in Indian cuisine, in which whole or ground spices are heated in hot oil or ghee and the mixture is added to a dish. Hot fat has an amazing ability to extract and retain the essence, aroma and flavor of spices and herbs."
— *Source: NPR, "The Crackling Spices of Indian Tempering," https://www.npr.org/2011/12/07/143251451/the-crackling-spices-of-indian-tempering*

**The Vegan School** (educational site for Indian vegan cooking) explains the science: "Tadka is the Indian technique of heating oil or ghee and adding whole spices to release their aromatic compounds. The hot fat extracts lipid-soluble flavour compounds from the spices, forming the dish's flavour base... The window between blooming and burning is about 10–20 seconds for most spices. Tadka requires attention."
— *Source: The Vegan School, "The Science of Indian Tadka," https://www.theveganschool.org/post/science-of-indian-tadka*

**Everyday Nourishing Foods** adds: "Tadka, or tempering, means frying spices in hot oil or ghee to release flavor. A plain dal, a bowl of rice, or a humble chutney suddenly feels complete with that sizzling spoonful of spices and oil."
— *Source: Everyday Nourishing Foods, "Tadka (Indian Tempering)," https://everydaynourishingfoods.com/tadka-indian-tempering/*

**Step-by-step technique:**

1. **Heat the oil or ghee** over medium heat. Don't let it smoke — if the oil smokes, it is too hot and will burn the spices.

2. **Add the whole spices first** (cardamom, cinnamon, cloves, bay leaves, cumin seeds). Fry 30–60 seconds. Listen for the sound — cumin seeds will "crackle" (pop and jump). When they release their aroma, they are ready.

3. **Then add the ground spices** (turmeric, chili, ground coriander, ground cumin). These burn faster — only 10–15 seconds. Stir constantly.

4. **Immediately add the next ingredient** (onion, tomato, or meat) to lower the temperature and prevent the spices from burning.

**Signs something went wrong:**
- If the spices turn black → they burned. Discard the oil and start over. Burned spices make the whole dish bitter.
- If you don't smell anything → the oil wasn't hot enough. Spices need heat to release their aroma.
- If the cumin seeds don't crackle → the oil isn't hot enough.

**Spice order (important):**
1. Hard whole spices first (cinnamon, cloves, cardamom, bay leaves) — they take longer to release their aroma
2. Seeds (cumin, mustard, fenugreek) — they crackle when ready
3. Ground spices last — they burn in seconds

**Golden rule:** It is better to have the oil slightly too cool and wait for the spices to release their aroma, than to have it too hot and burn them. You cannot recover from burned spices.

## 7. Mise en Place for Indian Cooking (Organizing Everything Before Starting)

**Critical for:** Cooking 5 dishes in a single day

Indian cooking requires you to work fast once you start. When spices are in hot oil, you have seconds to add the next ingredient — you don't have time to chop onion or look for the cumin. That is why **mise en place** (everything in its place) is essential.

**Indian As Apple Pie** (Indian cooking blog by Anupy Singla, author of Indian cookbooks) explains: "You've heard of the term mise en place, which is French for putting everything in its rightful place. Often we use this term ahead of a recipe — to ensure that you read the recipe through, gather the ingredients needed, and slice and dice them ahead of cooking."
— *Source: Indian As Apple Pie, "Amazing Meals Start with Prep: Mise en Place and Beyond," https://www.indianasapplepie.com/blogs/indian-as-apple-pie/amazing-meals-start-with-prep-mise-en-place-and-beyond*

**Posha** (Indian cooking and meal prep site) recommends: "The biggest time-saver is overlap: get a slow dal going, then chop and cook your sabzi while it simmers. Most people can get it done in two to three hours on a weekend afternoon."
— *Source: Posha, "What Is Indian Meal Prep? Beginner's Guide to Batch Cooking," https://www.posha.com/stories/indian-meal-prep-guide*

**How to organize your kitchen for Sunday cooking:**

### Saturday night (prep):
- [ ] Red kidney beans soaking in plenty of water (8-12 hours)
- [ ] Lamb marinating in yogurt + spices (overnight)
- [ ] Chicken marinating in tandoori yogurt (overnight)
- [ ] Cashews soaked and refrigerated

### Sunday morning — Mise en place (before turning on the stove):

**Chop and measure EVERYTHING before starting to cook.** Organize in small bowls:

**Group 1 — Whole spices (per dish):**
- Lamb korma: 5 cloves, 2-3 pieces of mace, 9 cardamom pods, 3 cinnamon sticks — in a small bowl
- Beef bhuna: 4 cardamom pods, 4 bay leaves, 3 cinnamon sticks, 1 pinch of fenugreek seeds — in another bowl

**Group 2 — Ground spices (measure and mix per dish):**
- Lamb korma: 3½ tsp ground coriander, 2⅓ tsp Kashmiri chili, 1¾ tsp turmeric, ⅝ tsp ground cumin — in one bowl
- Beef bhuna: 2 tsp Madras curry, 1 tsp garam masala, 1 tsp ground coriander, 1 tsp ground cumin, 1 tsp Kashmiri chili, 1 tsp chili powder, 1 tsp turmeric — in another bowl
- (Continue with each dish)

**Group 3 — Chopped aromatics:**
- Onions: chop and separate by dish (3 for beef bhuna, 2 for rajma, 3 for aloo gobi, 2 for lamb korma)
- Tomatoes: chop and separate (2 for beef bhuna, 5 for rajma, 5 for aloo gobi)
- Garlic: peel and chop (you need 4 heads total)
- Ginger: peel and grate (3 large pieces)
- Serrano chiles: chop (8-10)
- Cilantro: chop (3 bunches)

**Group 4 — Pastes and liquids:**
- Ginger-garlic paste (blend garlic + ginger + a little water)
- Cashew paste (blend soaked cashews + water)
- Greek yogurt (measure what you need for each marinade)
- Cream, butter, ghee — measure and separate

**Group 5 — Vegetables for aloo gobi:**
- Cauliflower: cut into medium florets
- Potatoes: cut into medium wedges

### Sunday cooking order (with overlap):

1. **First: start cooking the beans.** They take the longest (45-60 min). While they cook, you can do everything else.

2. **Second: start the lamb korma.** It needs 1 hour of simmering. While it cooks, you can prepare other things.

3. **Third: start the beef bhuna on another burner.** It also needs 1-1.5 hours. It can cook at the same time as the korma.

4. **Fourth: while the curries are cooking, put the tandoori chicken in the oven.** 30-35 minutes.

5. **Fifth: when the beans are done, make the rajma masala.** 30 minutes of active cooking.

6. **Sixth: make the aloo gobi.** 45 minutes. Fry the vegetables, make the masala, combine.

7. **Seventh: cook the basmati rice.** 20 minutes. Do this last so it's fresh.

8. **Finally: pack everything into containers.**

**Key tip:** If you have a slow cooker, use it for the beans or to keep something warm while you cook the rest. If you have an air fryer, use it for the aloo gobi vegetables (saves oil and time).
