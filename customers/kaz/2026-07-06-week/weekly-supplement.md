### Tahini (Sesame Paste)

**What it is:** It's a creamy paste made from ground sesame seeds, similar to how peanut butter is made from peanuts. It's a staple ingredient in all Middle Eastern cooking — used in hummus, sauces, and dressings.

**What it looks like:** A jar or can with a light beige-colored paste, similar to peanut butter but lighter. When first opened, it's usually separated: an oil layer on top and the dense paste below. You need to stir it well before using. Sold in glass jars or cans.

**What it tastes like:** It tastes like toasted sesame seeds — nutty, slightly bitter, but very rich. It's not sweet like peanut butter. Think of the flavor of toasted sesame seeds that get put on bread, but in soft paste form.

<img align="right" width="100" src="https://images-na.ssl-images-amazon.com/images/I/610zDYDLuBL.jpg">

**Where to buy in Playa del Carmen:**
- **Walmart** (30 Av. and Calle 8): Look in the international section or natural foods section. Ask for "tahini" or "sesame paste."
- **Chedraui** (Av. Juárez) and **Chedraui Selecto** (Plaza Las Américas): In the international/gourmet section. Selecto is more likely to have it.
- **DAC Market** (30 Av. between Calle 14 and 14 Bis): Best selection of Middle Eastern ingredients. You'll definitely find it here.

**Difficulty:** 🟡 medium

**If you can't find it:** You can make it at home. Toast 1 cup of sesame seeds in a dry pan over medium-low heat until golden (3-5 minutes, stirring constantly). Let them cool slightly. Put them in a blender with 2-3 tablespoons of olive oil and blend until you get a smooth paste (about 5 minutes, scraping the sides occasionally). Store in a jar in the fridge.

**⚠️ Gluten-Free:** Pure tahini is 100% sesame. Check the label: it should list "sesame seeds" as the only ingredient. Some cheap brands add wheat flour as thickener — avoid those.

### Ground Cardamom

**What it is:** A ground spice from a green pod originally from India, super common in Lebanese and Arabic cooking. Used in this week's shawarmas and kofta. It's one of the most expensive spices in the world after saffron.

**What it looks like:** A fine powder light brown/toasted color (like light cinnamon). If you buy it as whole pods, they're tiny green pods the size of a coriander seed.

**What it tastes like:** It has a floral and citrusy flavor, like a mix between lemon, eucalyptus, and a sweet touch. Very aromatic. In Lebanese cooking it gives that characteristic perfume that makes shawarma taste different from any other grilled meat.

<img align="right" width="100" src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/71J-6BE89SL.jpg">

**Where to buy in Playa del Carmen:**
- **Chedraui** (Av. Juárez): In the spice section, look for "ground cardamom" in a jar. McCormick brand sometimes has it.
- **DAC Market** (30 Av. between Calle 14 and 14 Bis): In bulk, cheaper and fresher than jarred. Ask for "cardamom ground."
- **Walmart** (30 Av. and Calle 8): In the spices/baking section. Less likely but worth checking.

**Difficulty:** 🟡 medium

**If you can't find it:** There's no perfect substitute because cardamom has a very unique flavor. If you absolutely can't find it, omit it — the dish will still taste good though it'll lose that characteristic aromatic note. Cinnamon is NOT a good substitute (totally different flavor).

**⚠️ Gluten-Free:** Pure ground spices don't have gluten. Buy from reputable brands (McCormick, Badia) or in bulk at DAC.

### Sumac

**What it is:** A deep red powdered spice made from ground dried berries. It's a classic ingredient in Lebanese and Turkish cooking. Used in this week's kofta to give an acidic touch.

**What it looks like:** A dark red/garnet powder, similar in color to ground dried chile but darker and more purple. The texture is slightly coarser than other ground spices, almost like ground flakes.

**What it tastes like:** It has a sour flavor, like lemon but without being citrusy. It's similar to the taste of tamarind or chamoy — that pleasant acidity that makes your mouth water. It's not spicy at all. In Lebanon they put it on the table the way we put salt or lemon.

<img align="right" width="100" src="https://images-na.ssl-images-amazon.com/images/I/81k8tStfElL.jpg">

**Where to buy in Playa del Carmen:**
- **DAC Market** (30 Av. between Calle 14 and 14 Bis): Your best bet. They have Middle Eastern spices and definitely have it. Ask for "sumac."
- **Amazon Mexico** (amazon.com.mx): Search for "sumac spice." Several brands sell it with shipping to Playa.

**Difficulty:** 🔴 difficult

**If you can't find it:** Mix ½ teaspoon lemon zest + ¼ teaspoon salt for every ¾ teaspoon of sumac the recipe calls for. It's not the same but gives that acidic and fresh touch that sumac provides.

**⚠️ Gluten-Free:** Pure spice, naturally gluten-free. Buy from a trusted source.

### Baharat (Lebanese 7-Spice)

**What it is:** A blend of 7 ground spices that's the backbone of Lebanese cooking. Every family has their own recipe, but it always includes allspice, black pepper, cinnamon, cloves, nutmeg, cardamom, and cumin. It's like the Middle Eastern "curry."

**What it looks like:** A dark brown/reddish powder. It looks like the color of ground ancho chile, but more brown. The aroma is warm, sweet, and spiced — when you smell it you'll think of Christmas desserts.

**What it tastes like:** Warm, slightly sweet, with notes of cinnamon and clove but with a savory, earthy background. NOT spicy. It's a flavor that reminds you of the Arabic food you've tried in restaurants.

<img align="right" width="100" src="https://www.acedarspoon.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/lebanese-seven-spice-blend-image-8.jpg">

**Where to buy in Playa del Carmen:**
- **DAC Market** (30 Av. between Calle 14 and 14 Bis): Your best bet. Look for "baharat" or "Lebanese 7 spice."
- **Amazon Mexico** (amazon.com.mx): Several brands available. Search for "baharat spice" or "Lebanese 7 spice."

**Difficulty:** 🔴 difficult

**If you can't find it — MAKE IT AT HOME:** This week you need about 1 teaspoon of baharat. Mix these ingredients (all available at any supermarket):

| Ingredient | Amount |
|------------|--------|
| Ground allspice | 1 teaspoon |
| Ground black pepper | 1 teaspoon |
| Ground cinnamon | ½ teaspoon |
| Ground cloves | ½ teaspoon |
| Ground nutmeg | ½ teaspoon |
| Ground cardamom | ½ teaspoon |
| Ground cumin | ½ teaspoon |

Mix everything and store in a sealed jar. Lasts for months. This recipe yields about 2 tablespoons and you only need 1 teaspoon for the beef shawarma — the rest you can use to season other meats or vegetables.

**⚠️ Gluten-Free:** All spices are naturally gluten-free. Verify they're pure, without fillers.

### Rice Vermicelli

**What it is:** A very thin and brittle noodle made 100% from rice flour. It's NOT the wheat vermicelli noodle we know in Mexico for soup. It's the same type of noodle used in Thai and Vietnamese cooking (like spring rolls or pho). For Lebanese rice we need rice vermicelli because the traditional version uses wheat vermicelli (which has gluten).

**What it looks like:** Comes in packages or plastic bags. They're white, thin, hard strands, like angel hair pasta but finer and translucent. Usually come in bundles or nests. Do NOT buy "glass noodles" — those are mung bean noodles and don't work for this.

<img align="right" width="100" src="https://hot-thai-kitchen.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/1.jpg">

**Where to buy in Playa del Carmen:**
- **Walmart** (30 Av. and Calle 8): In the Asian/international section. Look for Thai or Vietnamese brands like "Rice Vermicelli" or "Bun" or "Mai Fun." Sometimes found near the soy sauce.
- **Chedraui** (Av. Juárez): Same Asian section. More limited than Walmart but worth checking.
- **DAC Market** (30 Av. between Calle 14 and 14 Bis): Excellent selection of Asian products. You'll definitely find them here.

**Difficulty:** 🟢 easy

**If you can't find it:** Buy GLUTEN-FREE spaghetti and break it into 1-2 cm pieces with your hands before toasting. Barilla GF or similar brands work. Do NOT use regular wheat spaghetti — it contains gluten.

**⚠️ Gluten-Free:** It's rice, naturally gluten-free. But VERIFY the package: in Mexico some products labeled "fideo" are wheat. Look for "rice" in the ingredients. Asian rice noodles are the safest bet.

### Ground Lamb

**What it is:** Ground sheep meat. In Lebanese cooking it's mixed with ground beef to make kofta because lamb adds a juicier flavor and a characteristic aroma that beef alone doesn't have.

**What it looks like:** Ground meat darker red than beef, sometimes with white streaks of fat. Sold in trays in the meat section.

**What it tastes like:** It has a stronger and more "animal" flavor than beef, with a slightly sweet touch. When cooked with spices like the ones in the kofta, that strong flavor becomes delicious and juicy. If you've had lamb barbacoa or birria, it's that same flavor but in ground meat.

**Where to buy in Playa del Carmen:**
- **Walmart** (30 Av. and Calle 8): In the meat section. Sometimes they have packaged ground lamb.
- **Chedraui Selecto** (Plaza Las Américas): Best chance. They have a more extensive meat section with specialty cuts.
- **Chedraui** (Av. Juárez): Less likely, but check the cold meats section.

**Difficulty:** 🟡 medium

**If you can't find it:** Use 265g ground beef (80/20) and mix it with 1 tablespoon olive oil. The extra fat from the oil mimics the juiciness that lamb would provide. The flavor won't be identical but the kofta will turn out very good. This is the recipe's official substitution.

**⚠️ Gluten-Free:** Fresh ground meat doesn't contain gluten. If buying packaged meat, check that it doesn't have additives or added flavorings.

### Pine Nuts

**What it is:** Edible seeds from the cones (pine cones) of certain pine trees. They're a traditional ingredient in Lebanese rice and many Middle Eastern recipes. They're toasted to garnish the rice.

**What it looks like:** Tiny seeds, oval and elongated in shape, cream/pale ivory color, about the size of a fat grain of rice. They have a slightly darker tip at one end.

**What it tastes like:** Mild, buttery, and slightly sweet flavor. When toasted they develop a very rich nutty flavor and their texture becomes crunchy. It's like a finer, more elegant version of almonds, but milder.

### Allspice (Pimienta de Jamaica)

**What it is:** Despite the name, it's NOT a pepper and NOT spicy. It's a dried berry from a Caribbean tree that tastes like a combination of clove, cinnamon, and nutmeg — hence its English name "allspice" (all the spices). It's one of the most important ingredients in Lebanese cooking.

**What it looks like:** As whole berries they're small dark brown balls the size of a fat peppercorn. Ground, it's a dark reddish-brown powder, similar to dark cinnamon.

## PART 2 — COOKING TIPS

### A) How to Marinate Meat with Acid (Lemon and Vinegar)

**Why it works:** Lemon juice and vinegar are acids. When meat marinates in acid, the acid unwinds the meat's proteins (a process called denaturation). This makes the meat more tender and allows it to better absorb spice flavors. This is explained by Orka Technology in their article "The Chemistry of Tenderizing Meat" and confirmed by the scientific journal Foods in their 2023 review of marinades.

**What you need to know:**
- **Ideal time:** 3 to 24 hours for meats like the chicken and beef we're using this week. More than 24 hours and the acid starts to "cook" the meat and makes it mushy.
- **Always refrigerate:** Never marinate at room temperature. Bacteria grow fast. Always in the fridge.
- **Dry the meat before cooking:** When you take the meat out of the marinade, pat it dry with paper towels. If you put it in the pan wet, it'll steam instead of searing. We want that golden, crispy exterior.
- **Don't reuse the raw marinade:** The liquid where raw meat sat has bacteria. If you want to use the marinade as a sauce, boil it separately for at least 3 minutes.
- **Chicken marinates faster** (3-6 hours is enough) than beef (minimum 4, better overnight).

**For this week:** Marinate the chicken shawarma and beef shawarma on SUNDAY night. That way they're ready to cook Monday morning.

*Sources: Orka Technology — "The Chemistry of Tenderizing Meat: Enzymes, Acids, and Marinades" (https://www.orka.tech/en/the-chemistry-of-tenderizing-meat-enzymes-acids-and-marinades/); The Spice Girl Kitchen — "The Science of Marinating Meats and Vegetables" (https://thespicegirlkitchen.com/science-of-marinating/)*

### B) How to Peel Chickpeas for Creamy Hummus

**Why it matters:** The skin (husk) of chickpeas is the #1 reason homemade hummus turns out grainy/chalky instead of creamy like restaurant hummus. Canned chickpeas are already cooked and their skin is a thin, transparent film that wraps around them. If you remove it, the hummus comes out SILKY. This is explained by everyone from Deb Perelman of Smitten Kitchen to America's Test Kitchen.

**How to do it — 2 methods:**

**Method 1: Rub in warm water (recommended for this week — 2 cans)**
1. Drain and rinse the canned chickpeas.
2. Put them in a large bowl with warm water.
3. Put your hands in and rub the chickpeas against each other, like you're washing beans but more vigorously.
4. The thin skins will loosen and float to the surface.
5. Tilt the bowl and let the skins spill out with the water. Repeat 2-3 times until most skins are gone.
6. Drain the peeled chickpeas well.

**Method 2: With baking soda (faster but changes texture)**
1. Heat the rinsed chickpeas in a pan with 1½ teaspoon baking soda for 2-3 minutes.
2. Rinse with cold water 3-4 times, rubbing the chickpeas each time. The skins come off easily.

**Is it worth it?** YES. The difference between hummus with skins and without is HUGE. It takes 15-20 minutes for 2 cans. Do it while watching a show or listening to music — it's patience work but the result is worth every minute.

America's Test Kitchen, in their article "For Creamier Hummus, Skin Your Chickpeas" (https://www.americastestkitchen.com/cooksillustrated/how_tos/6634-for-creamier-hummus-skin-your-chickpeas), says: "Removing the tough skins from the garbanzo beans resulted in a far creamier end product."

### C) How to Toast Vermicelli Without Burning It (for Lebanese Rice)

**The risk:** Rice vermicelli goes from golden to burnt in seconds. If it burns, you have to throw it away and start over because the bitter flavor ruins the entire rice.

**Step-by-step technique:**
1. **Prepare everything BEFORE turning on the heat:** Have the rice already rinsed and drained nearby. This is important because when the vermicelli is ready, you need to add the rice immediately — there's no time to go looking for things.
2. **Medium heat, not high:** If the heat is too high, the vermicelli burns on the outside and stays raw inside. Medium heat lets you control the process.
3. **Don't stop stirring:** Use a wooden spoon or spatula and stir the vermicelli CONSTANTLY. Every 2-3 seconds. Pieces that stay still will burn.
4. **Look for dark golden color, not brown:** You want golden like a toasted tortilla, not brown like chocolate. When it starts to smell like toasted nuts, it's almost ready.
5. **Add the rice immediately:** As soon as the vermicelli is evenly golden, add the rice to the same pan. The cold rice helps lower the pan temperature and prevents the vermicelli from continuing to cook.

Blogger Yumna Jawad of Feel Good Foodie (Lebanese rice recipe — https://feelgoodfoodie.net/recipe/lebanese-rice/) says: "Stir frequently and keep a close eye on it so that it turns a deep golden brown without burning." Suzy Karadsheh of The Mediterranean Dish (https://www.themediterraneandish.com/lebanese-rice-with-vermicelli/) adds: "If it burns, you must throw the vermicelli away and start over."

### D) How to Form Kofta So They Don't Fall Apart

**Why they fall apart:** Ground meat without enough kneading doesn't develop bound protein (like a dough). If the mixture is too loose, the kofta logs crumble when cooking.

**Technique:**
1. **Process well:** The Mediterranean Dish recipe uses a food processor to mix EVERYTHING together (onion, garlic, parsley, meat, spices). This is intentional — the processor "kneads" the meat and develops a pasty texture that keeps the kofta together. Pulse until the mixture looks uniform and slightly sticky. Scrape down the sides and pulse again.
2. **Chill the mixture:** After processing, put the mixture in the fridge for 20-30 minutes. Cold fat firms up and the mixture becomes firmer and easier to handle. This also helps prevent it from breaking apart during cooking.
3. **Form uniform logs:** Take ~75g portions (about the size of a large croquette) and form logs about 7-8 cm long by 2.5 cm thick. They should all be the SAME thickness — if some are thick and others thin, they'll cook unevenly.
4. **Wet your hands:** Moisten your hands with a little water before forming each kofta. This prevents the meat from sticking to your fingers.
5. **Sear first:** When you put the kofta in the hot pan or on the baking sheet, do NOT move them for the first 3-4 minutes. Let a golden crust form. If you move them too soon, they can stick and fall apart.

**For this week:** Since we're not using bread (it's gluten-free), the mixture needs to be well-processed and cold. If when forming them the mixture feels too wet, refrigerate 10 more minutes. Suzy Karadsheh's recipe confirms that the kofta holds together without the bread.

*Source: Amira's Pantry — "Kofta Secrets: Soft & Juicy Meatballs" (https://amiraspantry.com/kofta-secrets-soft-juicy-meatballs/); Rana's Recipe — "Kofta Kebab Recipe" (https://ranasrecipe.com/recipe/kofta-kebab/)*

### E) How to Roast Potatoes Really Crispy

**For the Feel Good Foodie batata harra — the secret is in the preparation before the oven.**

**Technique:**
1. **Rinse the potato cubes:** After peeling and cutting the potatoes into 2 cm cubes, rinse them in a bowl with cold water. Change the water 2-3 times until it runs clear. This removes surface starch — starch makes potatoes stick together and get gummy instead of crispy.
2. **DRY the potatoes:** This is the step many skip and it's CRUCIAL. After rinsing, spread the potatoes on a clean kitchen towel and pat them dry. Wet potatoes = steamed potatoes, not crispy ones.
3. **Don't overcrowd:** Spread the potatoes in a SINGLE LAYER on the baking sheet. If they're stacked on top of each other, steam gets trapped and they won't brown. If your baking sheet isn't big enough, use TWO sheets.
4. **VERY hot oven:** The recipe calls for 230°C. Make sure the oven has reached that temperature before putting the potatoes in. A not preheated oven = sad potatoes.
5. **Don't move them too much:** Leave them 15-20 minutes on the first side without touching. Then flip them ONCE with a spatula. Flipping them several times breaks the crust that's forming.
6. **The baking soda trick (optional, from Serious Eats):** If you want EXTRA crispy potatoes, boil the potato cubes for 3-4 minutes in water with ½ teaspoon baking soda. Then drain, dry, and roast. The baking soda breaks down the potato surface and creates an extra crispy layer. It's not necessary for batata harra but it's a professional trick.

J. Kenji López-Alt of Serious Eats, in "The Best Crispy Roast Potatoes Ever" (https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-roast-potatoes-ever-recipe), explains: "Boiling potatoes in alkaline water with baking soda breaks down their exteriors, creating a starchy slurry that crisps up in the oven."

### F) How to Cut Meat into Thin Strips for Shawarma (Against the Grain)

**What "against the grain" means:** Meat is made up of long muscle fibers (like strands of thread). If you cut PARALLEL to those fibers, you get long strands that are hard to chew. If you cut PERPENDICULAR (against the grain), you cut the fibers into short pieces and the meat feels much more tender.

**How to identify the grain in beef (for beef shawarma):**
- Look at the raw piece of meat. You'll see lines or striations running in one direction, like the grain of wood. Those lines are the grain.
- The grain in beef fillet (shoulder/sirloin) is easy to see because these are cuts with long, defined fibers.

**Cutting technique:**
1. For beef shawarma, the recipe calls for cutting the meat into ½ cm strips before marinating.
2. Place the piece of meat on your board so the fibers run left to right (horizontal).
3. Now cut from top to bottom (vertical), PERPENDICULAR to the direction of the fibers.
4. Make strips maximum ½ cm thick. Thinner = more tender.
5. For chicken shawarma: the grain of chicken thigh is harder to see because it's softer meat. Simply slice the cooked thighs into 1 cm thick slices, perpendicular to the longest side of the thigh.

**Why it matters:** Napoleon Grills states it clearly: "Meat sliced with the grain instead of against it can be up to 4-times more chewy and tough." America's Test Kitchen explains: "Shorter lengths of muscle fibers are easier to chew."

*Sources: Napoleon Grills — "How and Why to Slice Meat Against the Grain" (https://www.napoleon.com/en/us/grills/blog/how-and-why-slice-meat-against-grain); America's Test Kitchen/Cook's Country — "Slicing Against the Grain: What It Means and How to Do It" (https://www.americastestkitchen.com/cookscountry/how_tos/12172-slicing-against-the-grain-what-it-means-and-how-to-do-it)*

### G) Mise en Place for Batch Cooking

"Mise en place" is a French phrase that means "everything in its place." It's what professional chefs do: have ALL ingredients measured, chopped, and ready before turning on the stove.

**For this week — prepare on SUNDAY night:**

**Marinate (Sunday night):**
- [ ] Marinate the chicken shawarma in its bag/container. Into the fridge.
- [ ] Cut beef into ½ cm strips and marinate the beef shawarma. Into the fridge.
- [ ] Make the tahini sauce (3 minutes). Into the fridge in a small jar.
- [ ] If making homemade baharat, mix it and store it.

**Monday morning — before turning anything on:**
- [ ] Take the marinated meats out of the fridge 1 hour before so they lose the chill.
- [ ] Rinse the basmati rice until water runs clear. Leave it draining.
- [ ] Peel and cut the potatoes into 2 cm cubes. Rinse them and dry them.
- [ ] Chop everything: onions, tomatoes, garlic, parsley, and cilantro. Separate what goes to each recipe in bowls or little piles.
